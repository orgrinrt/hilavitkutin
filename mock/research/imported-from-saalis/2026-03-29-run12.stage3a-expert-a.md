## My Disagreement

I disagree with the Savant's claim that my combined replication approach
"domesticates the replication lattice by treating it as a simple budget
parameter." The Savant writes:

> "A flat 'budget = 1' in Active/Stress reduces crystal growth to a
> constant drip feed, ignoring the supersaturation."

This is a mischaracterization. My Stage 2a combined approach is not a
flat budget. It is a budget floor combined with probabilistic
deduplication and priority ordering. The budget = 1 is the floor, not
the ceiling. In Idle mode my budget is 5 chunks per tick. The Savant's
supersaturation-weighted growth rate is a genuine improvement over my
floor, and I adopt its scaling property. But the Savant's framing
implies I proposed a constant drip feed across all states, when in fact
my Stage 2a proposal already scaled across metabolic states (Background
= 1, Idle = 5, Active = 1, Stress = 1). The Savant's contribution is
making the rate within each state proportional to deficit. That is
valuable. The framing that I treated the replication lattice as "a
simple budget parameter" is inaccurate; I treated it as a combined
mechanism with three components.

That said, the Savant's supersaturation formula exposes a real deficiency
in my Active/Stress budget of 1. The 39-day replication time for a 2 GB
blob at 585 bytes/second is a legitimate failure mode. I accept the
supersaturation-weighted growth rate and integrate it below.

## Section 1: WiFi-Aware Scar Attenuation (Three-Layer Defect Classification)

### 1.1 Convergence Analysis

The Savant proposes three scar layers: Thermal (WiFi-induced, 1-7 day
decay), Structural (genuine faults, 180-day decay), and Grain Boundary
(relay/path faults, 30-day decay). The classification is determined at
scar creation time by the diagnostic triangle's output.

From a convergence perspective, this mechanism is a CRDT-compatible
extension of the existing scar GSet. The GSet remains append-only. The
scar entries gain a new field (ScarAttribution variant). The read-time
decay function branches on the attribution type to select the decay
period. No existing CRDT invariant is violated:

1. The GSet grows monotonically. New attribution variants do not remove
   entries.
2. The merge function for GSets is set union. Union of scars with
   different attributions produces a superset containing all scars. No
   conflict.
3. The decay function is a pure function of (scar, current_time). It
   is evaluated at read time. Two replicas computing the decay function
   on the same scar at the same wall-clock time produce the same result,
   because the decay period is determined by the attribution variant
   (which is part of the scar entry, not local state).

The three-layer classification is therefore a valid CRDT extension. It
does not introduce new conflict resolution requirements.

### 1.2 Partition Behavior with Different WiFi Conditions

This is the critical question. During a network partition, two sides of
the mesh may experience different WiFi conditions. Side A has good WiFi
(quality 0.95). Side B has bad WiFi (quality 0.60). Each side generates
scars independently during the partition.

**Side A behavior:** Side A sees all heartbeats from its local peers.
Inter-cluster connections to Side B are down (partition). Side A
generates no WiFi-induced scars for local peers (good WiFi). It
generates no scars for Side B peers (no heartbeats received means no
observation, and partition is detected via the convergence mechanism,
not the scar system).

**Side B behavior:** Side B has bad WiFi. Intra-cluster heartbeats are
lossy. With the Savant's widened threshold (30 consecutive misses for
scar generation during WifiTransient diagnosis), most WiFi bursts are
absorbed. If a burst exceeds 30 seconds, Side B generates WifiInduced
scars with wifi_quality = 0.60, producing a 1 + (0.60 * 6) = 4.6 day
decay period.

**After partition heals:** Convergence Front 1 merges the GSet. Side A
receives Side B's WifiInduced scars. Side B receives Side A's clean
record. The merged GSet contains all scars from both sides. The
dominance cascade now includes Side B's WifiInduced scars, but these
decay in 4.6 days, not 180.

**The convergence question:** Do the two sides agree on the dominance
ordering after GSet merge? Yes, because:

1. Both sides compute dominance from the merged GSet (post-convergence,
   the GSets are identical).
2. The decay function is deterministic: same scar, same epoch, same
   result.
3. The attribution is stored in the scar entry, not derived from local
   WiFi conditions at computation time.

This last point is critical. The Savant's design stores the
wifi_quality_at_observation in the WifiInduced variant. This means the
decay period is determined by the WiFi quality at the time the scar was
created, not the WiFi quality at read time. A peer on Side A (good WiFi)
reads a WifiInduced scar from Side B and computes the same decay as
Side B does, because both read the same wifi_quality_at_observation
value from the scar entry.

**Verdict:** The three-layer classification converges correctly after
partition heal. The attribution is observation-time state embedded in
the CRDT entry, not query-time local state. This is the right design.
GSets with richer payloads are well-studied (Shapiro et al., 2011); the
merge semantics are unaffected by payload complexity.

### 1.3 Edge Case: WiFi Quality Oscillation During Scar Generation

There is one edge case the Savant does not address. The diagnostic
triangle produces PathDiagnosis values that can change between
heartbeat ticks. If the WiFi quality oscillates around the 0.75
threshold during a scar-generating event (30 consecutive misses), the
PathDiagnosis may be WifiTransient at miss 15 and DestinationFault at
miss 25 (because WiFi quality momentarily recovered).

The Savant's classify_scar function takes the diagnosis at scar
creation time (miss 30). But the diagnosis at miss 30 may not reflect
the diagnosis during the majority of the event. If WiFi was bad for
25 of the 30 missed heartbeats but recovered just before the scar
threshold, the scar would be classified as Structural (DestinationFault)
instead of Thermal (WifiInduced).

**My correction:** The scar should capture the minimum WiFi quality
observed during the observation window, not the instantaneous WiFi
quality at scar creation time. This requires the health observation
pipeline to track min_wifi_quality over the current observation window.
The observation window starts when the first missed heartbeat occurs
and ends when either (a) the scar threshold is reached, or (b)
heartbeats resume (window closes without scar). The min_wifi_quality
is a local running minimum, reset when the observation window closes.

```
struct ObservationWindow {
    consecutive_misses: u16,
    min_wifi_quality_q8_8: u16,  // minimum wifi_quality during this window
    window_start_epoch: u64,
}

fn on_heartbeat_miss(window: &mut ObservationWindow,
                     current_wifi_quality: u16) {
    window.consecutive_misses += 1;
    if current_wifi_quality < window.min_wifi_quality_q8_8 {
        window.min_wifi_quality_q8_8 = current_wifi_quality;
    }
}

fn on_scar_threshold_reached(window: &ObservationWindow)
    -> ScarAttribution
{
    // Use min_wifi_quality over the entire observation window
    let threshold_q8_8 = 0x00C0; // 0.75 in Q8.8
    if window.min_wifi_quality_q8_8 < threshold_q8_8 {
        ScarAttribution::WifiInduced {
            observer: my_dk,
            wifi_quality_at_observation: window.min_wifi_quality_q8_8,
        }
    } else {
        ScarAttribution::DirectObservation {
            observer: my_dk,
            subject: peer_dk,
        }
    }
}
```

This running minimum ensures that a scar is classified as WifiInduced
if WiFi was bad at any point during the observation window, even if it
recovered before the threshold was reached. The min_wifi_quality is
local state (not replicated); it affects only the attribution field of
the scar entry that is eventually written to the GSet. Once written,
the attribution is immutable and replicated.

The running minimum is coordination-free: each peer independently
tracks its own observation windows and generates scar entries with
locally-determined attributions. No cross-peer state is needed for
classification. The replicated state (GSet entries) contains the
observation-time WiFi quality, which all peers use identically at
read time.

### 1.4 Taking It Further: Scar Attribution Consensus

The three-layer classification raises a subtle consistency question
that neither the Savant nor I have addressed. Two peers may observe the
same event (e.g., peer C stops responding) and generate different scar
attributions because their local WiFi quality differs. Peer A (good
WiFi) diagnoses DestinationFault and generates a Structural scar
(180-day decay). Peer B (bad WiFi) diagnoses WifiTransient and
generates a Thermal scar (4-day decay).

After GSet merge, both scars exist. The dominance cascade processes
both: a 180-day scar from Peer A and a 4-day scar from Peer B, both
about the same subject (Peer C). This is correct behavior. The GSet
does not deduplicate by subject; it accumulates all observations. The
two scars are independent observations from different vantage points.
Peer A's observation is "Peer C is unreachable and my WiFi is fine,
so Peer C has a problem." Peer B's observation is "Peer C is
unreachable but my WiFi is also bad, so this might be my WiFi."

Both observations are valid. The dominance cascade should weight them
independently. The 180-day scar from Peer A correctly penalizes Peer C
(because Peer A ruled out WiFi). The 4-day scar from Peer B correctly
attenuates (because Peer B could not rule out WiFi). The net dominance
impact is somewhere between the two, which accurately reflects the
diagnostic uncertainty.

This is not a consistency problem. It is the system correctly
representing epistemic uncertainty in the CRDT structure. Two observers
with different information produce different but compatible assessments.
The GSet union preserves both. The dominance cascade computes a
weighted aggregate. No consensus is needed; the CRDT semantics handle
it.

## Section 2: Supersaturation-Weighted Replication

### 2.1 Interaction with Anti-Entropy Reconciliation

The Savant's supersaturation-weighted growth rate replaces my flat
budget with a deficit-proportional rate:

```
base_rate = match metabolic {
    Background => 2, Idle => 5, Active => 3, Stress => 1
};
scaled = (base_rate * saturation_q16) >> 16;
rate = max(scaled, 1);
```

This interacts with anti-entropy reconciliation (Merkle root comparison
in heartbeats) in two ways.

**Interaction 1: Stale filter data.** The supersaturation calculation
requires accurate replica counts, which come from the content filter.
The content filter is reconciled via the anti-entropy mechanism. Before
reconciliation completes, the replica counts are stale. The
supersaturation value may be wrong: a blob that appears to have 0
replicas (high supersaturation) may actually have 2 replicas on the
other side of the partition (the filter has not reconciled yet).

The Savant's Mechanism 4 (convergence-gated replication start) addresses
this by gating replication on filter reconciliation completion. This is
correct. I analyze the gating mechanism in Section 3 below.

**Interaction 2: Anti-entropy during supersaturation-driven replication.**
Once the filter is reconciled and replication begins, each successful
transfer changes the replica count. The replica count change must be
reflected in the content filter and propagated via the next heartbeat's
Merkle root. The anti-entropy mechanism detects the divergence (new
replica not yet reflected in the remote peer's filter). This triggers
incremental Merkle reconciliation, which updates the remote peer's
filter.

The timing matters. The supersaturation calculation runs on every
heartbeat tick (2 seconds at Active). A transfer completes (changing
the local filter). The Merkle root changes. The next heartbeat
advertises the new root. The remote peer detects divergence and
reconciles. The remote peer's supersaturation calculation now reflects
the new replica. Total propagation time: 2-4 heartbeat intervals (4-8
seconds at Active, 10-20 seconds at Idle).

During this propagation window, both peers may independently decide to
replicate the same blob. Peer A creates a replica on Peer C. Peer B
has not yet seen the updated filter (propagation delay). Peer B also
creates a replica of the same blob on Peer D. The blob now has 1 extra
replica above target.

**Does this create replication divergence?** No. Over-replication is
safe. The system converges to exactly target_replicas replicas
eventually: once the filter propagates the extra replica, the
supersaturation drops to 0, and no peer initiates further transfers.
The probabilistic roll (my contribution) already mitigates this: the
BLAKE3 roll is deterministic per (blob_id, peer, epoch), so two peers
with the same stale filter data do not both roll "replicate" with
probability 1. The expected over-replication is bounded by 1/(known_
holders + 1) extra replicas per propagation window.

**Verdict:** Supersaturation-weighted replication does not create
replication divergence. It may create transient over-replication
(bounded by 1 extra replica per propagation window per blob), which
resolves when the anti-entropy mechanism propagates the filter update.
This is the standard eventual consistency property of epidemic
replication (Demers et al., 1987).

### 2.2 My Extension: Priority-Weighted Supersaturation

The Savant notes that the supersaturation calculation "normalizes by
target, so 1/3 missing on P1 has the same urgency as 1/1 missing on
P4." The Savant acknowledges this may not be correct and suggests
multiplying by a priority weight.

I formalize this. The supersaturation should incorporate a priority
multiplier that increases the effective supersaturation for higher-
priority matter:

```
fn replication_growth_rate(
    deficit: u8,
    priority: MatterPriority,
    metabolic: MetabolicState,
) -> u8 {
    let target = priority.target_replicas();
    let saturation_q16 = (deficit as u32) << 16 / (target as u32);

    let priority_weight_q16 = match priority {
        P1 => 0x00030000,  // 3.0x
        P2 => 0x00020000,  // 2.0x
        P3 => 0x00010000,  // 1.0x
        P4 => 0x00008000,  // 0.5x
    };

    let weighted_saturation = (saturation_q16 as u64
        * priority_weight_q16 as u64) >> 16;
    // Cap at 1.0 (0x10000) to prevent overflow in subsequent math
    let capped = if weighted_saturation > 0x10000 {
        0x10000u32
    } else {
        weighted_saturation as u32
    };

    let base_rate = match metabolic {
        Background => 2u32,
        Idle => 5,
        Active => 3,
        Stress => 1,
    };

    let scaled = (base_rate * capped) >> 16;
    scaled.max(1) as u8
}
```

This means a P1 blob with 1/3 missing has effective supersaturation
3.0 * (1/3) = 1.0, while a P4 blob with 1/1 missing has effective
supersaturation 0.5 * (1/1) = 0.5. P1 under-replication gets 2x the
growth rate of P4 under-replication, even though both have the same
raw deficit ratio. This captures the design intent that P1 content is
more critical.

### 2.3 Supersaturation Interaction with the Probabilistic Roll

The supersaturation rate and the probabilistic roll are independent
mechanisms that compound. The roll determines whether this peer
initiates replication at all. The rate determines how many chunks per
tick if replication proceeds. The expected replication throughput for
a given blob is:

```
E[chunks/tick] = P(roll) * growth_rate
               = (deficit / (known_holders + 1)) * growth_rate
```

For a P1 blob at 0 replicas, known_holders = 1 (the originator),
deficit = 2 (target 3, current 1):

```
P(roll) = 2 / (1 + 1) = 1.0  (always replicate when deficit >= holders)
growth_rate = max(1, (3 * (2/3 * 3.0)) >> 16) = max(1, 3 * 2) >> ... = 3
E[chunks/tick] = 1.0 * 3 = 3 chunks/tick in Active mode
```

At 1170 bytes per chunk and 2-second ticks (Active): 3 * 1170 / 2 =
1755 bytes/second. A 2 GB blob takes ~13.2 days. Still slow, but this
is under Active mode resource pressure. In Idle mode (5 * 2 = 10 chunks/
tick, 5-second intervals): 10 * 1170 / 5 = 2340 bytes/second, or ~9.9
days.

These numbers reveal that the chunk-based budget is fundamentally
limited by the small chunk size (1170 bytes) and the heartbeat-tick
granularity. A dedicated transfer session (TransferSession with bao
streaming) is the correct mechanism for bulk replication, not the
per-tick chunk budget. The supersaturation rate should control how many
concurrent transfer sessions to initiate, not how many individual chunks
to send. Each transfer session runs its own LEDBAT flow.

**My correction to the Savant's model:** The replication growth rate
should be denominated in concurrent transfer sessions, not in chunks
per tick. The growth rate determines how many new TransferSessions to
open per tick. Each TransferSession manages its own bao chunk stream
at LEDBAT pace. The supersaturation controls the parallelism, not the
per-session throughput.

```
fn replication_sessions_to_open(
    deficit: u8,
    priority: MatterPriority,
    metabolic: MetabolicState,
) -> u8 {
    // Same supersaturation calculation as above
    let growth = replication_growth_rate(deficit, priority, metabolic);
    // But growth is now "new sessions to open this tick"
    // Cap by metabolic state's max concurrent sessions
    let max_sessions = match metabolic {
        Background => 1,
        Idle => 3,
        Active => 2,
        Stress => 1,
    };
    growth.min(max_sessions)
}
```

This preserves the Savant's supersaturation-driven scaling while
connecting it to the actual transfer mechanism (TransferSessions with
LEDBAT pacing). A P1 blob at 0 replicas in Idle mode can open up to
3 concurrent transfer sessions to different target peers. Each session
runs at LEDBAT pace. The aggregate replication throughput is bounded
by the LEDBAT controller, not by an artificial chunk budget.

## Section 3: Convergence-Gated Replication Start

### 3.1 Deadlock Analysis

The Savant proposes a gate predicate:

```
REPLICATION_READY = filter_reconciliation_complete
                    AND convergence_front >= Front2
```

The concern is whether this creates a deadlock with convergence fronts.
Specifically: can a situation arise where convergence cannot complete
because replication is gated, and replication cannot start because
convergence has not reached Front 2?

**Analysis of the convergence front sequence:**

- Front 1: control log merge + dominance recompute. This is GSet merge
  and pure computation. No dependency on replication.
- Front 2: KeyBundle + polygon CPSK renegotiation. This depends on
  control log merge (Front 1) and inter-cluster connectivity. No
  dependency on replication.
- Front 3: re-encryption (P1 4h/P2 24h/P3 bandwidth/P4 epoch-forward).
  This depends on key distribution (Front 2) and blob access. Blobs
  must be readable for re-encryption. Re-encryption reads the existing
  ciphertext, decrypts with the old key, re-encrypts with the new key.
  This does not require the blob to be replicated; it requires the blob
  to exist at the re-encryption peer's territory on the hash ring.

**Conclusion: no deadlock.** The convergence fronts do not depend on
replication. Fronts 1 and 2 are control-plane operations (GSet merge,
key renegotiation). Front 3 is a data-plane operation (re-encryption)
that operates on existing blobs, not on newly-replicated blobs.
Replication of new content discovered during filter reconciliation is
a separate concern from re-encryption of existing content with new keys.

The REPLICATION_READY gate blocks new replication transfers until
Front 2 completes and filters are reconciled. During this time,
convergence proceeds normally (Fronts 1, 2, 3). After the gate opens,
replication begins with accurate filter data. The gate and convergence
are independent state machines that share a triggering event
(reconnection) but do not create circular dependencies.

### 3.2 Subtlety: Filter Reconciliation and Front 3 Overlap

There is a timing subtlety. Filter reconciliation runs concurrently
with convergence (my Stage 2a Section 6.6 says: "After ControlGate
opens (Front 1 complete), concurrent with Fronts 2-3, asynchronous
completion"). The REPLICATION_READY gate requires filter_reconciliation_
complete. If filter reconciliation is slow (many divergent entries,
network congestion), the gate may open late in the convergence process,
potentially after Front 3 has already started.

This is acceptable. Replication is independent of re-encryption. A peer
can re-encrypt existing blobs (Front 3) while waiting for filter
reconciliation to complete. Once reconciliation completes and the gate
opens, replication begins alongside ongoing Front 3 re-encryption.
Both are data-plane operations using bulk-tier streams and LEDBAT
pacing. The Conductor's bandwidth governance ensures they share the
available bandwidth without starving each other.

### 3.3 My Extension: The Replication Warmup Ramp

The Savant identifies that the gate creates a step function: replication
goes from 0 to full supersaturation rate in one tick. The Savant
proposes a 3-tick warmup ramp. I endorse this but modify the details.

The ramp should be governed by the Conductor, not by a hardcoded 3-tick
schedule. The Conductor already knows the system's resource state. When
the REPLICATION_READY gate opens, the Conductor should treat the
replication subsystem as a new resource consumer entering the system.
The Conductor's meet projection already handles multiple resource
dimensions. Adding replication bandwidth to the projection means the
metabolic state naturally absorbs the new load.

The practical mechanism: when REPLICATION_READY transitions from false
to true, the replication subsystem starts with max_sessions = 1
regardless of the supersaturation rate. Each subsequent tick, the
Conductor evaluates resource pressure. If pressure remains below the
Active threshold, max_sessions increases by 1. If pressure crosses
Active, max_sessions holds. This is a feedback-controlled ramp, not
a fixed 3-tick schedule. On a powerful device, the ramp reaches full
rate in 2-3 ticks. On a Pi Zero under load, the ramp holds at 1
session until resource pressure subsides.

```
fn ramp_replication_sessions(
    tick_since_gate_open: u16,
    metabolic: MetabolicState,
    supersaturation_sessions: u8,
) -> u8 {
    // Exponential ramp: 1, 2, 4, ... capped at supersaturation rate
    let ramp = 1u8.checked_shl(tick_since_gate_open.min(7) as u32)
        .unwrap_or(u8::MAX);
    let ramped = ramp.min(supersaturation_sessions);

    // Conductor override: if we are in Active/Stress, cap harder
    let cap = match metabolic {
        Background => 1,
        Idle => supersaturation_sessions,
        Active => 2,
        Stress => 1,
    };
    ramped.min(cap)
}
```

### 3.4 Interaction with the Savant's Temperature Model

The Savant describes convergence fronts as temperature stages: Front 1
is high temperature (all state mobile), Front 2 is medium temperature
(topology changes), Front 3 is low temperature (bulk data). The
REPLICATION_READY gate opens at >= Front 2, which is medium temperature.

From a distributed systems perspective, the temperature model maps
cleanly to a happens-before ordering:

- Front 1 events happen-before Front 2 events.
- Front 2 events happen-before Front 3 events.
- Replication events happen-after the REPLICATION_READY gate, which
  requires Front 2 completion and filter reconciliation.

The causal ordering is: GSet merge (Front 1) -> CPSK renegotiation
(Front 2) -> filter reconciliation completion -> replication start.
This is a valid partial order with no cycles. The temperature model
and the causal ordering are isomorphic. The Savant's crystallographic
language maps to a standard distributed systems concept (causal
ordering of convergence phases).

## Section 4: Diagnostic Path Triangle

### 4.1 State and Coordination-Free Constraint

The Savant proposes a PathTriangle structure that holds three health
measurements (direct_health, relay_health, wifi_quality) and produces
a PathDiagnosis via a pure function (8-way match on three boolean
thresholds).

**Does this add state?** Yes. The PathTriangle holds three HealthScore
/ CapacityRatio values. These are local state (not replicated). Each
peer maintains its own PathTriangle for each inter-cluster connection.
The PathTriangle is computed from locally-observed SACK ratios and
locally-measured WiFi quality.

**Does this violate coordination-free operation?** No. The PathTriangle
is a local computation. The diagnose() function is pure: it reads
three local values and produces a local classification. No cross-peer
communication is needed to produce a diagnosis. The diagnosis
influences local decisions (scar classification, relay failover, NAT
keepalive). These local decisions produce CRDT entries (scars) that are
replicated via the GSet. But the diagnosis itself is never replicated.

This is the correct separation. The diagnosis is an intermediate
computation. The CRDT entries are the replicated artifacts. The
diagnosis influences the content of CRDT entries (scar attribution)
but is not itself a CRDT. This is analogous to how a hash function is
not replicated, but the hash value is included in the replicated
message.

**Coordination-free property preserved:** Two peers may produce
different diagnoses for the same event (Peer A diagnoses
DestinationFault, Peer B diagnoses WifiTransient). Each produces a
scar with different attribution. Both scars enter the GSet. No
coordination is needed to resolve the different diagnoses. The GSet
union preserves both, and the dominance cascade weights them
independently. This is precisely the CALM theorem in action:
monotonic computations on CRDTs require no coordination (Hellerstein,
2010).

### 4.2 False Diagnosis Risk

The diagnostic triangle has a failure mode that the Savant does not
analyze. The wifi_quality measurement is local. It reflects the local
WiFi link quality. But the "direct_health" measurement includes WiFi
quality implicitly: if the local WiFi is bad, all direct paths are
degraded. The diagnostic triangle's logic assumes the three dimensions
are independent:

```
(direct good, relay good, wifi bad) => WifiTransient
```

But if WiFi is bad, the relay path also traverses WiFi (on the local
leg). The relay_health measurement should also be degraded. The
diagnosis should be:

```
(direct bad, relay bad, wifi bad) => LocalLinkDown
```

The WifiTransient diagnosis (direct OK, relay OK, WiFi bad) is
unlikely in practice because WiFi degradation affects all paths. The
only scenario where WifiTransient applies is when WiFi quality dropped
momentarily (too brief to degrade the EMA-smoothed direct_health and
relay_health scores) but is now bad (the instantaneous measurement).

This means the WifiTransient diagnosis is a transient state during the
EMA convergence window. The EMA smoothing creates a lag: wifi_quality
drops instantly, direct_health drops over several heartbeats (EMA
decay). During the lag, the diagnosis is WifiTransient. After the EMA
catches up, the diagnosis transitions to LocalLinkDown.

**My correction:** The diagnostic triangle should include a temporal
stability requirement. A diagnosis should persist for at least 3
consecutive evaluations (3 heartbeat ticks) before triggering
automated responses. This prevents the transient WifiTransient
diagnosis during EMA convergence from prematurely attenuating scar
generation:

```
fn stable_diagnosis(
    current: PathDiagnosis,
    history: &[PathDiagnosis; 3],
) -> PathDiagnosis {
    if history.iter().all(|h| *h == current) {
        current
    } else {
        PathDiagnosis::AllHealthy // default: no action
    }
}
```

The AllHealthy default during instability means no scar attenuation and
no automated path changes during the first 3 ticks of a diagnosis
change. This is conservative: it avoids acting on transient states at
the cost of 3 heartbeat intervals of delayed response (6 seconds at
Active, 15 seconds at Idle). Given that scars have multi-day impact,
a 15-second response delay is negligible.

### 4.3 Taking It Further: Triangle as Partition Detector

The diagnostic triangle provides information that the current design
does not exploit: the ability to distinguish partitions from peer
failures.

Current design: when heartbeats stop arriving from a remote cluster, the
mesh assumes partition. It starts the convergence wavefront timer.

With the triangle: when heartbeats stop arriving:
- direct_health drops. relay_health drops. wifi_quality stays high.
  Diagnosis: DestinationFault. The remote cluster or its gateway is
  down.
- direct_health drops. relay_health stays high (the relay is alive and
  forwarding probes). wifi_quality stays high. Diagnosis: NatExpired.
  Send keepalive. No partition; just a NAT mapping timeout.
- direct_health drops. relay_health drops. wifi_quality drops.
  Diagnosis: LocalLinkDown. The local device lost connectivity. This
  is not a remote partition; this is local failure.

These distinctions matter for convergence triggering. A NatExpired
diagnosis should trigger NAT keepalive renewal, not convergence. A
LocalLinkDown diagnosis should delay convergence (the local device
cannot participate in convergence while its link is down). Only a
DestinationFault or confirmed partition (both direct and relay
paths down, local WiFi OK) should trigger convergence.

This is a concrete improvement to the convergence wavefront timer's
trigger condition. Currently, the timer fires "on reconnection." With
the triangle, the trigger becomes: "on reconnection after a
DestinationFault diagnosis that persisted for >= partition_threshold
seconds." NatExpired and LocalLinkDown do not trigger convergence;
they trigger their respective remediation paths.

**The PARTITION_ANN question:** OQ-3 in the digest asks for the trigger
condition for the PARTITION_ANN control log entry type. The diagnostic
triangle provides the answer: PARTITION_ANN is emitted when the
diagnosis is DestinationFault for >= partition_threshold seconds AND
remediation attempts (NAT keepalive, relay failover) have failed.
This is a deterministic, locally-computable trigger condition. The
PARTITION_ANN entry is appended to the GSet control log. After
partition heals, the GSet merge distributes the PARTITION_ANN to all
peers, providing a historical record of the partition.

## Section 5: The 1-Chunk Minimum Budget and the Savant's Concession

### 5.1 The Savant's Concession

The Savant concedes in Section 5 of the Stage 2b:

> "Active/Stress replication halt is wrong. My Stage 1 specified
> replication_budget = 0 in Active/Stress. Expert A correctly identifies
> the post-convergence deadlock."

The Savant replaces the halt with supersaturation-weighted growth with
minimum rate >= 1. This is functionally identical to my "budget is
NEVER zero" correction, expressed through a different mechanism. The
Savant's mechanism is superior to my flat minimum because it scales
with deficit. I adopt the Savant's supersaturation formula and extend
it with my priority weighting (Section 2.2) and session-based
granularity (Section 2.3).

### 5.2 Does the 1-Chunk Minimum Survive?

Yes, but it survives as a mathematical floor of the supersaturation
formula, not as an explicit parameter. The Savant's formula produces:

```
scaled = (base_rate * saturation_q16) >> 16;
rate = max(scaled, 1);
```

The `max(scaled, 1)` is the 1-chunk minimum. It appears as a
consequence of the `max` call, not as a design decision. But it is
the same guarantee: replication rate is never zero in any metabolic
state.

In my session-based reformulation (Section 2.3), the minimum becomes
1 session. Even in Stress mode, the peer opens at least 1 transfer
session for the most under-replicated blob. That session runs at
LEDBAT pace, yielding to all foreground traffic. The throughput is
whatever LEDBAT allows, which may be near-zero under genuine Stress
but is never exactly zero (LEDBAT has a minimum rate of 1 segment
per RTT).

The 1-session minimum survives because it is the irreducible unit of
replication progress. You cannot make progress with 0 sessions. You
can make progress with 1 session, however slowly. The Savant's
concession and my original correction converge to the same design
point: minimum 1 unit of work per tick, scaled by supersaturation
above that minimum.

### 5.3 The Savant's Relay-First Split

The Savant proposes a split that I did not anticipate: relay-first for
control plane, hole-punch-first for data plane. This is interesting
from a replication perspective. Replication transfers are data plane
(bulk tier, LEDBAT). Under the Savant's split, replication would
prefer direct paths (hole-punched) over relay paths.

This is correct for throughput: direct paths have lower latency and
higher bandwidth than relay paths. But it creates a bootstrapping
problem: the replication gate opens after Front 2, which is when CPSK
renegotiation completes. At this point, the relay-first control plane
is already established. If the data plane prefers hole-punched paths,
the replication session must wait for hole punching to succeed before
transferring.

**My recommendation:** Replication should start on the relay path (which
is already established) and migrate to the direct path when hole
punching succeeds. This is the same strategy as the overall connectivity
model (relay-first, direct-upgrade), applied to replication specifically.
The TransferSession supports path migration via PATH_CHALLENGE (same
mechanism as connection migration). The replication does not wait for
hole punching; it starts immediately on the available path and upgrades
later.

Integrating the Savant's mechanisms with my corrections:

### 6.1 Replication Protocol (updated from Stage 2a Section 6.1)

- **Priority ordering:** most_under_replicated() (Savant)
- **Probabilistic deduplication:** BLAKE3 roll, Q16.16 fixed-point (mine)
- **Supersaturation-weighted rate:** deficit-proportional growth (Savant),
  with priority weighting (my extension, Section 2.2)
- **Session-based granularity:** growth rate controls concurrent
  TransferSessions, not individual chunks (my correction, Section 2.3)
- **Convergence gate:** REPLICATION_READY = filter_reconciliation_complete
  AND convergence_front >= Front2 (Savant Mechanism 4, endorsed)
- **Feedback-controlled warmup:** ramp from 1 session, Conductor-governed
  (my extension, Section 3.3)
- **Minimum rate:** 1 session in all metabolic states (convergent with
  Savant's min rate >= 1)
- **Cluster-diversity placement:** at least 2 clusters for target >= 3
  (Savant)
- **Path selection:** start on relay, migrate to direct when available
  (my recommendation, Section 5.3)
- **Relay-held replicas:** count toward targets, territory delegated to
  nearest non-relay peer on hash ring (Stage 2a Section 6.4)

### 6.2 Scar Classification (new, from Savant's mechanisms)

  (180-day decay), Grain Boundary (30-day decay) (Savant Mechanism 3)

- **WiFi quality capture:** minimum WiFi quality over observation window,

  consecutive_misses counter + min_wifi_quality_q8_8 running minimum
- **CRDT compatibility:** attribution stored in GSet entry payload;
  read-time decay function branches on attribution type; GSet merge

### 6.3 Diagnostic Path Triangle (new, from Savant's mechanism)

- **Three dimensions:** direct_health (EMA), relay_health (EMA),

- **Pure diagnosis function:** 8-way match, Q16.16 threshold 0.75
  (Savant)
- **Temporal stability:** 3-tick persistence before automated action
  (my correction, Section 4.2)
- **Coordination-free:** diagnosis is local; only scar entries are
  replicated (my analysis, Section 4.1)
- **Partition detection:** triangle informs PARTITION_ANN trigger
  condition, distinguishing partition from NAT expiry and local link
  failure (my extension, Section 4.3)

### 6.4 Convergence Wavefront Trigger (updated)

Previous: "on reconnection."
Updated: "on reconnection after DestinationFault diagnosis persisted

## Section 7: Remaining Questions for the Project Lead

1. **WiFi quality measurement mechanism.** The PhysicalBoundary gate
   for WiFi quality is unspecified. How does the OS-level WiFi RSSI or
   delivery ratio enter the fixed-point abstract state machine? This
   needs a new PhysicalBoundary newtype (WifiQuality) and a defined
   sampling interval. The Savant identifies this as an amorphous region
   (Section 6, item 4) but does not propose a concrete mechanism.

2. **Supersaturation rate calibration.** The base_rate values
   (Background = 2, Idle = 5, Active = 3, Stress = 1) are initial
   guesses. These need empirical calibration against the deployment
   envelope (Pi Zero to NAS, 5 Mbps to 1 Gbps). The calibration items
   table in verkko-ops should include these 4 values.

3. **Observation window reset semantics.** When a heartbeat resumes
   after a miss streak below the scar threshold, the observation window
   closes. Should the consecutive_misses counter reset to 0
   immediately, or decay (e.g., decrement by 1 per successful
   heartbeat)? Immediate reset means a pattern of 29 misses, 1 success,
   29 misses, 1 success never generates a scar (never reaches 30).
   Gradual decay means the counter erodes slowly and eventually reaches
   30 during sustained intermittent loss. I lean toward immediate reset
   (conservative, avoids false scars) but this should be a calibration
   item.

4. **Grain boundary scar target.** The Savant's three-layer
   classification says grain boundary scars have "moderate dominance
   impact on the relay, not on the destination peer." But the relay is
   a pseudo-peer. Does the relay participate in the dominance cascade?
   If not, grain boundary scars need a different dominance target.
   Perhaps the grain boundary scar reduces the relay's health score
   (separate EMA track, as the Savant proposes), which influences relay
   selection (failover to warm standby) but does not affect the
   destination peer's dominance score.
