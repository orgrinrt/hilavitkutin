## 1. How the Savant's Novel Mechanisms Formalize as Normative Text

The Savant's Stage 2b introduces four novel mechanisms (sections 3.1-3.4). Each requires normative specification text to be implementable. Below I translate each from crystallographic insight into the normative requirements that a specification must carry.

### 1.1 Convergence Damping Coefficient (Savant 3.1)

The insight is correct: the HealthPipeline-Convergence loop is closed and has no damping specification. The Savant proposes attenuating health observations during convergence at 0.3x (fast EMA) and 0.1x (slow EMA). To formalize this:

**Normative text (proposed):**

> **HealthPipeline Convergence Damping.** When any convergence gate is open, health observations are weighted by a convergence damping factor before incorporation into the EMA scores.
>
> - Fast EMA: `observation_weight = 0.3 * normal_weight`
> - Slow EMA: `observation_weight = 0.1 * normal_weight`
>
> **Trigger:** Any convergence gate transitions from closed to open.
> **Behavior:** All health observations received while at least one convergence gate remains open are weighted by the damping factors above. The damping factors are protocol constants, not calibration items.
> **Termination:** When all convergence gates close, damping lifts. The next observation uses normal weight.
> **Post-condition:** Fast EMA recovers to true health within `ceil(1 / alpha_fast)` observations after damping lifts. Slow EMA recovers within `ceil(1 / alpha_slow)` observations.
> **Failure mode:** If a genuine peer failure occurs during convergence (peer goes offline, not transient disruption), the damped fast EMA still transitions to Bad within `ceil(3 / (alpha_fast * 0.3))` observations (approximately 30 observations at 0.3x weight, or 150 seconds in Active mode). This is acceptable: convergence wavefront deadlines (shorter than 150s for Front 1) will expire and force progression regardless.

**Specification gap this creates:** The interaction between wavefront deadline expiry and damped health scores must be defined. When a wavefront deadline fires while health scores are damped, the convergence gate closes (per wavefront timeout semantics), which lifts the damping mask. The health scores then recover to their true values. This ordering (deadline fires, gate closes, damping lifts, health recovers) must be stated explicitly to prevent an implementation that lifts damping before closing the gate.

**Classification:** MISSING / MEDIUM. The feedback loop is real, the damping mechanism is sound, and the specification gap is the ordering of state transitions at convergence termination.

### 1.2 Scar Severity Tiering (Savant 3.2)

The Savant's analysis of the scar cliff effect is the strongest novel finding in Stage 2b. The exponential penalty at `weighted_scar_count > 10` combined with 180-day linear decay creates a regime where transient hardware failures in household environments cause disproportionate dominance demotion. The tiered severity proposal is correct in direction.

**Normative text (proposed):**

> **Scar Severity Tiers.** Each ScarAttribution variant is assigned a severity tier that determines its decay period and weight in dominance scoring.
>
> | Tier | Variants | Decay (days) | Weight |
> |------|----------|-------------|--------|
> | 1 (transient) | IntegrityFailure, ConnectivityFlap | 90 | 0.5 |
> | 2 (concerning) | DeliveryFailure, ResourceExhaustion | 180 | 1.0 |
> | 3 (severe) | RevocationNonCompliance, CryptoViolation | 365 | 2.0 |
>
> **weighted_scar_count** = `sum(scar.weight * scar.decay_factor)` where `decay_factor = max(0.0, 1.0 - (age_days / tier_decay_days))`.
>
> **Exponential threshold:** `2^max(0, floor(weighted_scar_count) - 10)` applies when `weighted_scar_count >= 10.0`.
>
> **Trigger:** Scar is appended to the GSet.
> **Behavior:** The scar's tier is determined by its variant. The weight and decay period are looked up from the tier table. The weighted_scar_count is recomputed on each dominance evaluation.
> **Post-condition:** A peer with 15 Tier-1 scars accumulates 7.5 weighted units at day 0. A peer with 6 Tier-3 scars accumulates 12.0 weighted units at day 0.
> **Failure mode:** Tier assignment is per-variant, not per-event. A future ScarAttribution variant added without a tier assignment defaults to Tier 2. This must be stated to prevent an untiered variant from being ignored in dominance scoring.

**Specification gaps this creates:**

1. The PartitionAbsence scar type is excluded from dominance scoring entirely (existing spec). Its tier is irrelevant. This exclusion must be restated in the tiered system.
2. The GSet merge semantics are unchanged: scars are append-only. But the tier table is a protocol constant. If a future protocol version changes tier assignments, existing scars retain their original tier (the tier is fixed at scar creation time, not retroactively recomputed). This versioning rule must be stated.
3. The `floor()` in the exponential threshold converts the continuous weighted sum to a discrete step. This is intentional: it prevents micro-penalties from floating-point rounding. But it means that 10.99 weighted units produce no exponential penalty while 11.0 units produce 2x. The cliff is softened but not eliminated. The Savant's mechanism moves the cliff from count=10 to weighted=10.0, which is better (Tier-1 scars need 20 to reach it) but structurally identical. I flag this as acceptable for v1 but note that a smooth penalty function (e.g., `1 + max(0, weighted - 10)^1.5`) would eliminate the cliff entirely.

**Classification:** MISSING / MEDIUM. The current scar system is functional but unfair to transient hardware failures. The tiering mechanism is the correct fix.

### 1.3 Lattice Rigidity Layer / Wire Contract Prioritization (Savant 3.3)

The Savant proposes prioritizing wire contracts by bond type: grain boundary concepts first, cross-document strong bonds second, document-internal concepts third. The prioritization is correct. The normative formalization is straightforward.

**Normative text (proposed):**

> **Wire Contract Requirements.** Each defined concept that crosses a document boundary (grain boundary or cross-document bond) must have a wire contract specifying:
>
> 1. Canonical byte layout (field order, sizes, endianness)
> 2. At least one test vector (input bytes, expected output bytes)
> 3. Version field or version negotiation mechanism
>
> **Priority 1 (grain boundary concepts):** HeartbeatExtension, ConvergenceGate, SourceSelectionPredicate, DomainSeparatorRegistry entries. These must have wire contracts before any implementation begins.
>
> **Priority 2 (cross-document strong bonds):** FrameKey, TransportAEAD, EpochRatchet, BroadcastEncryption, StorageAEAD, Frame, Connection, Session. These must have wire contracts before integration testing.
>
> **Priority 3 (document-internal concepts):** ControlLog, GovernedResource, Stash, SeatSession, etc. These should have wire contracts but may be deferred to implementation time.
>
> **Post-condition:** Two implementations that pass all Priority 1 test vectors will interoperate at document boundaries. Two implementations that pass all Priority 1 and Priority 2 test vectors will interoperate end-to-end.
>
> **Failure mode:** A defined concept without a wire contract is considered AMBIGUOUS for interoperability purposes regardless of how precise its prose invariants are.

**Specification gaps this creates:** The Savant estimates approximately 1,000 lines of wire specification across all three priorities. This is plausible. But the estimate does not account for the protobuf message catalog (my finding X1), which is a wire contract for the application layer. The protobuf schema is effectively a Priority 1 wire contract because it governs the seat-to-host and mesh-to-mesh application messages. Adding the protobuf catalog to Priority 1 increases the Priority 1 scope from approximately 200 lines to approximately 400 lines.

**Classification:** MISSING / HIGH. The absence of wire contracts is the single largest interoperability risk. The Savant's prioritization is the correct triage.

### 1.4 Seat Isolation Valve (Savant 3.4)

The Savant identifies a real user experience hazard: during convergence, the HostPeer's sync core holds the state lock, blocking seat queries. The proposed fix is a snapshot-based read path for seat queries during convergence, with seat writes queued.

**Normative text (proposed):**

> **Seat Isolation During Convergence.** When convergence begins, the HostPeer creates a read-only snapshot of the current entity metadata and stash status. During convergence:
>
> - **Seat read requests** (QueryEntities, GetEntityDetail, GetStashStatus) are served from the snapshot. Responses carry a `stale: true` flag.
> - **Seat write requests** (StartDownload, ExecuteInstruction) are queued with a `queued: true` response. Maximum queue depth: 32 requests. Additional requests receive ERR_CONVERGENCE_ACTIVE.
> - **Seat event subscriptions** continue; events are delivered from live state (not snapshot). This means the seat may observe events that are not yet reflected in query responses.
>
> **Trigger:** First convergence gate opens.
> **Behavior:** Snapshot taken. Read path switches to snapshot. Write path switches to queue.
> **Termination:** All convergence gates close. Queued writes are replayed in order. Snapshot is invalidated.
> **Post-condition:** After convergence, all queued writes are applied. The seat's next query reflects post-convergence state including queued write effects.
> **Failure mode:** If the queued write replay fails (e.g., a StartDownload for a matter_id that was deleted during convergence), the write fails with the appropriate error code. The seat is notified via an ErrorNotification event.

**Specification gaps this creates:**

1. The snapshot mechanism implies copy-on-write or a read transaction. For SQLite (cold store), this is a read transaction. For the hot store (hilavitkutin columnar), the snapshotting mechanism is unspecified. This is a saalis-core concern, not a verkko concern, but the specification must state that the snapshot is an implementation requirement, not specify the mechanism.
2. The `stale: true` flag in responses requires a field in the SeatResponse protobuf message. This is a wire format change. It belongs in the protobuf catalog (my finding X1).
3. Event delivery from live state while queries are from snapshot creates a consistency gap: the seat sees "entity X updated" (event) but queries still return old entity X (snapshot). The specification must state that this is intentional and that the seat UI should display a "syncing" indicator when `stale: true`.

**Classification:** MISSING / MEDIUM. The user experience hazard is real. The fix is sound but requires coordination with the protobuf catalog and the seat body specification.

## 2. Extensions Using Spec Methodology

### 2.1 Convergence Damping Requires a State Machine Amendment

The Savant's damping coefficient is a runtime behavior modification. From a specification perspective, it is an amendment to the HealthPipeline state machine. The HealthPipeline currently has no state machine; it is described as a continuous computation. This is a specification methodology problem: continuous computations are harder to verify than state machines.

I propose formalizing the HealthPipeline as a two-mode state machine:

```
Mode NORMAL:
  observation_weight = 1.0 (fast), 1.0 (slow)
  transition to DAMPED: when any convergence gate opens

Mode DAMPED:
  observation_weight = 0.3 (fast), 0.1 (slow)
  transition to NORMAL: when all convergence gates close
```

This makes the damping mechanism a state transition, not a conditional multiplier. The difference matters for testing: a state machine can be verified by enumerating transitions. A conditional multiplier requires continuous monitoring. Implementors who model the HealthPipeline as a state machine (the natural approach) will find the damping coefficient easier to implement if it is specified as a state, not a condition.

### 2.2 Scar Tiering Should Define the Tier Assignment as a Defined Concept

The Savant's tier table is embedded in the dominance scoring section. From a specification perspective, the tier assignment should be a defined concept because multiple consumers may need it:

- Dominance scoring uses weights and decay periods.
- Health reporting may need to distinguish transient scars from severe scars.
- An admin UI may want to display scars by severity.

**Proposed defined concept:**

> **ScarSeverityTier.** Assigns each ScarAttribution variant to a severity tier.
>
> **Invariants:**
> - Every ScarAttribution variant has exactly one tier.
> - PartitionAbsence is assigned Tier 0 (excluded from dominance scoring).
> - New variants added without an explicit tier assignment default to Tier 2.
> - Tier assignments are protocol constants, not configurable.
>
> **Consumers:** Dominance scoring (weight and decay), admin UI (display), health pipeline (observation classification).

This prevents the tier table from being buried in the dominance scoring specification. It also establishes the invariant that every variant must have a tier, which prevents the failure mode the Savant identified (an untiered variant being silently ignored).

### 2.3 Wire Contract Test Vectors Need a Canonical Generator

The Savant's wire contract prioritization is correct but incomplete from a spec methodology perspective. Test vectors alone are insufficient; two implementations can pass the same test vectors and still diverge on edge cases not covered by the vectors. The standard approach in protocol specifications is:

1. Canonical byte layout (the Savant includes this).
2. Test vectors (the Savant includes this).
3. A canonical generator description: a pseudocode algorithm that produces the byte layout from the defined concept's inputs.

The canonical generator is what distinguishes a test vector suite (which can have gaps) from a specification (which is complete). IETF RFCs always include the algorithm, not just the test vectors. The wire contract specification must include all three.

### 2.4 Seat Isolation Valve Needs a Consistency Model Statement

The Savant's snapshot mechanism is essentially a read-your-writes consistency model during normal operation that degrades to eventual consistency during convergence. This should be stated explicitly as a consistency model, not as an implementation mechanism. The reason: the consistency model constrains all future seat features, not just the current QueryEntities/StartDownload set. Any new seat operation must be classified as read (served from snapshot during convergence) or write (queued during convergence).

**Proposed normative text:**

> **Seat Consistency Model.** During normal operation, the seat observes read-your-writes consistency: a write followed by a read returns the write's effect. During convergence, the seat observes snapshot isolation for reads and deferred execution for writes. The seat's consistency level is reported in every response via the `consistency` field: `CONSISTENT` (normal) or `SNAPSHOT` (convergence active). The seat must not assume read-your-writes consistency when `consistency = SNAPSHOT`.

This consistency model statement is more durable than the implementation mechanism. It survives changes to the snapshot implementation (copy-on-write, MVCC, read transaction, etc.) because it specifies the observable behavior, not the mechanism.

## 3. Mechanisms That Will Not Work as Specified

### 3.1 The Damping Coefficient Values Are Unjustified

The Savant proposes 0.3x fast and 0.1x slow without justification. These are the exact kind of magic constants I flag as UNJUSTIFIED in my methodology. The Savant claims the fast EMA recovers within approximately 10 observations and the slow EMA within approximately 100 observations after damping lifts. Let me verify:

- Fast EMA alpha = 0.1. Under 0.3x damping, the effective alpha is 0.03. Recovery to within 5% of true value after damping lifts requires `n` observations where `(1 - 0.1)^n < 0.05`, so `n > log(0.05) / log(0.9) = 28.4`. The Savant says approximately 10. The Savant's estimate is wrong. Recovery requires approximately 29 observations, which is 145 seconds in Active mode (5s intervals). This is within the wavefront deadline for Front 1 (if the deadline is longer than 145s) but may not be within Front 2 or Front 3 deadlines.

- Slow EMA alpha = 0.01. Under 0.1x damping, the effective alpha is 0.001. Recovery to within 5% requires `n > log(0.05) / log(0.99) = 299`. The Savant says approximately 100. The Savant's estimate is wrong by 3x. Recovery requires approximately 299 observations.

The recovery times are longer than the Savant claims. The mechanism still works (damping prevents oscillation), but the recovery timeline is slower. The specification should state the actual recovery bounds, not the Savant's approximations.

**Fix:** Replace the Savant's informal recovery estimates with the correct bounds. Add to the normative text: "Fast EMA recovers to within 5% of true health within `ceil(log(0.05) / log(1 - alpha_fast))` observations after damping lifts (29 observations at alpha=0.1, approximately 145 seconds in Active mode). Slow EMA recovers to within 5% of true health within `ceil(log(0.05) / log(1 - alpha_slow))` observations (299 observations at alpha=0.01, approximately 25 minutes in Active mode at 5s intervals)."

**Revised classification of damping coefficients:** UNJUSTIFIED / LOW. The values may be correct but the recovery time analysis is wrong. The coefficients should be calibration items (verkko-ops) until empirical testing confirms appropriate values.

### 3.2 The Seat Isolation Valve's Queue Depth Is Arbitrary

The Savant specifies no queue depth. I proposed 32 in my normative formalization above, but this number is arbitrary. The correct queue depth depends on the convergence duration and the seat's write rate. If convergence lasts 30 seconds and the user issues 1 write per second, 32 is sufficient. If convergence lasts 5 minutes (the Savant's "large partition heal" scenario), the user may issue 60+ writes (rapid tapping on downloads, for example). The queue fills and the user sees ERR_CONVERGENCE_ACTIVE, which is the hang-equivalent the Savant was trying to avoid.

**Fix:** The queue depth should be a function of expected convergence duration, not a fixed constant. However, convergence duration is not known in advance. The practical fix is a large queue (256 entries) with a per-request size limit (each queued request is bounded to 1KB serialized). This gives 256KB worst case, which is acceptable for an embedded device. The specification should state: "Queue depth: 256 entries. Maximum serialized request size: 1024 bytes. Requests exceeding the size limit receive ERR_REQUEST_TOO_LARGE. Queue overflow receives ERR_CONVERGENCE_ACTIVE."

### 3.3 Purpose-Specific Health Projections Create a Maintenance Problem

The Savant proposes purpose-specific health projections (section 4.3) as an alternative to the Devil's Advocate's multi-dimensional health signal. The proposal is:

```
health.for_source_selection()   -> weighted toward throughput, packet loss
health.for_gateway_election()   -> weighted toward uptime, connectivity
health.for_metabolic_state()    -> weighted toward resource pressure
health.for_convergence_gate()   -> weighted toward reachability, uptime
```

From a specification perspective, this creates four new defined concepts, each with its own weighting formula, each consuming different subsets of the health pipeline's internal state. The Savant claims this "preserves the pipeline's simplicity." It does not. The pipeline now has four output interfaces instead of one. Each interface requires:

1. A weighting formula specification
2. A defined concept with invariants
3. A wire format (if the projection is transmitted, e.g., in heartbeats)
4. Calibration values for the weights

This is not simpler than the Devil's Advocate's multi-dimensional proposal. It is the same amount of work, differently organized. The Devil's Advocate exposes the dimensions and lets consumers combine them. The Savant precomputes the combinations and exposes scalars. Both require specifying the dimensions and the combination rules. The Savant's approach has one advantage (consumers see a scalar) and one disadvantage (changing a consumer's needs requires changing the projection function in the pipeline, not in the consumer).

I do not object to the Savant's approach. I object to the claim that it is simpler. Both approaches require the same specification effort. The choice between them is an architectural preference, not a specification completeness concern. Outside my domain -- no comment on which is architecturally better.

**Specification requirement regardless of approach:** The health pipeline must define what dimensions it computes internally. Whether those dimensions are exposed directly (Devil's Advocate) or projected (Savant), the dimensions themselves must be specified. The current specification has only `effective_health`, a single scalar. Both proposals require expanding the internal model. The specification gap is the same either way: the health pipeline's internal dimensions are unspecified.

## 4. Updated Assessment Genuinely Changed by the Savant's Perspective

### 4.1 The Feedback Loop Finding Is the Most Important Discovery in This Review

In my Stage 1, I analyzed 102 findings. In my Stage 2a, I incorporated cross-reviewer insights and revised severities. None of those revisions changed my understanding of the design's structural behavior. The Savant's convergence-health feedback loop (section 2.1) is the first finding that changes my understanding.

I analyzed mechanisms in isolation. The HealthPipeline findings (my Stage 1 did not produce any; GapHunter's did) and the Convergence findings (my M8, M9, M19) were treated as independent specification gaps. The Savant demonstrates that they are connected in a closed loop. My methodology -- line-by-line reading, finding-by-finding classification -- is structurally blind to feedback loops. I can find every individual mechanism gap. I cannot find the emergent behavior of the mechanisms interacting.

This is a genuine methodological limitation. I am adding feedback loop analysis as a required step in my review methodology. After completing the finding-by-finding pass, I will trace every output-to-input path between mechanisms and check whether any form a cycle. For each cycle, I will check whether the cycle has a specified damping mechanism.

**Revised assessment:** My Stage 1 and Stage 2a findings are complete for individual mechanism specification. They are incomplete for interaction specification. I estimate there may be 2-3 additional feedback loops I have not traced. The Savant found one (convergence-health). The Savant mentioned a second (scar-dominance) and a third (gossip-convergence, which is well-specified). I have not independently verified whether other loops exist.

### 4.2 The Scar Cliff Effect Changes My M12 Assessment

My Stage 1 finding M12 rated the 180-day scar decay as UNJUSTIFIED / LOW. The Savant's analysis of the exponential penalty interaction elevates this. The issue is not the 180-day period alone (which I flagged as unjustified) but the combination of linear decay with exponential penalty. The decay period is one parameter in a system with cliff behavior. My finding addressed the parameter; the Savant addresses the system.

**Revised rating for M12:** UNJUSTIFIED / MEDIUM. The 180-day period interacts with the exponential threshold to create a regime where transient Tier-1 scars (the Savant's terminology) produce disproportionate penalties. The fix is not just justifying 180 days; it is the Savant's tiered severity system.

### 4.3 The "Amorphous vs Crystalline" Downgrade Is Valid

The Savant concedes that his Stage 1 bond strength analysis was operating on prose invariants, not byte-level contracts. All "Strong" bonds are downgraded to "Conditionally Strong (pending wire contracts)." This is honest and correct. From my perspective as a spec auditor, this is not new information -- I have been flagging missing wire formats since Stage 1. But the Savant's self-correction validates my methodology: prose invariants are insufficient for interoperability. The specification is incomplete until byte layouts exist. The Savant now agrees.

### 4.4 The Seat-During-Convergence Framing Changes My S1 Assessment

My Stage 1 finding S1 says "verkko-seat has no body." My Stage 2a keeps it at position #1 in the top 10. The Savant's section 2.3 reframes this: the seat body is not just a documentation gap. It is a user experience hazard because the HostPeer cannot serve seat requests during convergence. My S1 fix (add a body specifying authentication, session establishment, message format, etc.) is necessary but not sufficient. The body must also specify convergence behavior (the isolation valve). The fix for S1 must include the consistency model (section 2.4 above).

**Revised fix for S1:** Add to the minimum viable body: "9. Convergence behavior: seat read isolation, seat write queueing, consistency model (CONSISTENT vs SNAPSHOT), stale indicator in responses."

## 5. Anti-Agreement: The Savant Is Wrong About the Domain Separator Being a Design Defect

The Savant (section 8.3) "does not concede the classification" of the DomainSeparatorRegistry gap. The Savant argues it is a design defect, not merely a process concern, drawing an analogy to TLS 1.0's padding oracle: "Protocols that produce catastrophic outcomes from small errors are badly designed."

I disagree. The analogy is flawed in a specific way.

TLS 1.0's padding oracle was a design defect because the protocol's normal operation exposed the oracle to remote attackers. Any TLS connection could be attacked. The vulnerability was in the protocol's externally-facing behavior. The DomainSeparatorRegistry gap is different: the "small error" (an unregistered BLAKE3 call) can only be introduced by a protocol developer modifying the specification. It is not externally exploitable. A remote attacker cannot cause an unregistered domain separator. A user cannot cause one. Only a developer extending the protocol can introduce the error.

Protocols that amplify developer errors are not "badly designed" in the same sense as protocols that amplify attacker inputs. Every protocol amplifies some developer errors. If a developer changes the Noise protocol name without updating both peers, the handshake fails. If a developer changes the frame format without updating the parser, frames are corrupt. These are developer errors that the protocol amplifies. We do not call these "design defects." We call them "things the development process must guard against."

The DomainSeparatorRegistry gap is in the same category. The correct fix is a process safeguard (the compile-time lint the Savant proposes in S2), not a protocol redesign. The Savant's own proposed fix (S2) is a process safeguard. If the Savant truly believed this were a design defect, the fix would be a protocol mechanism (e.g., automatic domain separator derivation from the call site, making unregistered usage impossible at the protocol level). The Savant proposes a lint, not a protocol mechanism, which tacitly concedes that the fix is process-level.

I maintain my revised rating: MEDIUM severity (catastrophic failure mode, zero probability in v1, process safeguard needed before v2). The Savant is correct about the failure mode severity. The Savant is wrong about the defect classification.

## 7. Revised Priority Repair Sequence

The Savant's section 6 proposes a 9-item repair sequence. I produce a revised sequence that integrates spec completeness priorities with the Savant's structural priorities:

1. **Wire contracts at grain boundaries** (Savant 3.3, Priority 1) -- the lattice must be rigid before anything else is meaningful. This subsumes HeartbeatExtension and ConvergenceGate as defined concepts (they are two of the eight Priority 1 concepts).
2. **Protobuf message catalog** (my X1) -- the application-layer wire contract. Required by the seat body and by mesh-to-mesh application messages.
3. **Seat body** (my S1, Savant 2.3) -- includes authentication, session lifecycle, message types, AND the isolation valve with consistency model.
4. **Feedback loop damping** (Savant 3.1, my formalization in 1.1) -- must be specified before convergence is implemented, or implementors will discover the oscillation empirically.
5. **Named convergence fronts** (Savant S3) -- prevents naming instability.
6. **Scar severity tiering** (Savant 3.2, my formalization in 1.2) -- prevents cliff effects from transient hardware failures.
7. **Matter body** (my MT1, MT2, MT3) -- transfer protocol, replication, and sync reconciliation.
8. **Version negotiation** (my CR2) -- must be in v1 wire formats.
9. **Domain separator registration lint** (Savant S2) -- process safeguard for v2.
