## Section 2: What They All Missed -- Domestications and Structural Holes

### Domestication 1: The Polling Model Lost Its Structural Guarantee

My Stage 1 proposed a two-phase feed/poll model where reentrancy is
prevented by the calling convention: you cannot call feed() from
within output processing because poll_output() does not call back.
The separation is structural, enforced by the protocol's API shape,
not by a prose contract or a borrow checker that only works in Rust.

Expert B formalizes this into a four-method trait with `&mut self` on
all methods. He then argues that Rust's borrow checker provides the
same reentrancy prevention. He is correct for Rust. He is wrong for
the specification.

Verkko is a protocol. The specification must be implementable in C,
in Go, in any language. The borrow checker argument is a Rust
implementation detail. My structural reentrancy prevention works in
any language: the protocol only produces outputs through poll_output;
poll_output does not accept inputs; therefore the consumer cannot
feed inputs during output processing. This is a property of the API
shape, not of any language's type system.

Expert B filed this edge down because Rust makes it unnecessary. But
the specification is not a Rust program. The specification is a
protocol. The protocol's reentrancy guarantee should be language-
independent.

**The emergent behavior that was lost:** When the polling model is
structurally non-reentrant, the consumer's state transitions are
sequential and predictable. Feed produces state change. Poll drains
consequences. The mesh layer processes consequences. Then the next
feed. This sequential discipline creates a deterministic evaluation
order for the entire system. Health scores update after heartbeats.
Convergence gates evaluate after health updates. Replication
decisions follow convergence gates. The ordering is not specified
per mechanism; it falls out of the sequential feed/poll discipline.
In Expert B's four-method model, the ordering between poll_transmit
and poll_event is specified by convention ("drain transmissions
first"). In my model, the ordering between all outputs is specified
by the single drain loop: outputs emerge in the order the protocol
generated them, which is the causal order.

Expert B's transmit-before-event ordering is good for SACK latency.
I concede the WiFi scenario where SACK delay causes spurious
retransmissions. But the fix is not to split the drain into two
loops. The fix is to have the protocol produce SendBytes outputs
before application events in the single drain, which it already does
(the protocol processes the frame, generates the SACK immediately,
enqueues it, then generates the HeartbeatReceived event). The causal
ordering of the single drain already provides transmit-first
behavior without splitting the API.

**My revised position:** I maintain the two-phase feed/poll model
for its structural reentrancy guarantee. I concede Expert B's
arena-epoch batching argument (see Section 3 for the revised arena
rule). I do not concede the four-method split.

### Domestication 2: Replication Growth Lost Its Crystal Physics

My Stage 1 proposed a crystal growth algorithm: most_under_replicated
determines where the crystal is thinnest; replicas grow at the
thinnest face. Expert A formalized this by adding a probabilistic
per-peer roll and a fixed budget. The probabilistic roll is a genuine
improvement. The fixed budget is a domestication.

Crystal growth rate depends on supersaturation, not on a budget. In
a real crystal, a region with high supersaturation (many dissolved
atoms, few lattice sites) grows faster than a region with low
supersaturation. The budget approach treats all under-replication
equally: 1 chunk per tick whether the blob has 0 replicas or 2/3
target replicas. The supersaturation approach grows faster where the
deficit is larger.

Expert A's probabilistic roll already captures some of this (the
probability scales with deficit/known_holders). But the budget
caps the growth rate independent of the supersaturation. At budget
= 1 in Active mode, a peer with 100 P1 blobs at 0 replicas
replicates one chunk per tick. The supersaturation is 100x and the
growth rate is 1.

Nobody noticed this mismatch because nobody modeled the replication
system as a growth process. They modeled it as a work queue.

### Domestication 4: Convergence Temperature Lost Its Physical Meaning

My Stage 1 described convergence fronts as heating stages: Front 1
is high temperature (flood gossip, GSet merge, EMA reset), Front 2
is medium temperature (dominance recomputation, ring coloring),
Front 3 is low temperature (bulk re-encryption work).

Expert A formalized this as a four-phase gateway handoff protocol.
The temperature analogy was dropped. What was lost: the temperature
is not decoration. In a crystal, the annealing temperature determines
which defects can move and which are frozen. At high temperature
(Front 1), all state is mobile: control log entries merge, scars
merge, epoch counters reconcile. At medium temperature (Front 2),
topology changes: gateway succession resolves, CPSK sessions
renegotiate. At low temperature (Front 3), only bulk data moves:
re-encryption proceeds, replicas restore.

The physical meaning constrains what operations can occur in each
front. If you introduce a new mechanism (e.g., WiFi quality
estimation), you must ask: at what temperature does this mechanism
operate? WiFi quality estimation is a local measurement; it operates
at all temperatures. But WiFi-induced scar decay adjustment is a
state mutation; it operates at Front 1 temperature (when scar state
is mobile). If you try to adjust scar decay during Front 3 (low
temperature), the scar state is frozen and the adjustment fails.

Nobody used the temperature model to sequence the devil's advocate's
proposed WiFi-aware adjustments. They should.

### Structural Hole 2: Arena Exhaustion During WiFi Burst Is Unaddressed

The devil's advocate identifies that 500 WiFi-buffered frames
overflow the hot arena (64 KB). Expert B's calling convention handles
ArenaExhausted by drain-reset-retry. The devil's advocate calculates
16 drain-reset cycles to process 500 frames.

Nobody addresses the interaction between the drain-reset cycles and
the nonce persist stall. The devil's advocate identifies nonce
persist stalls (4-20ms per persist on SD card) separately from arena
overflow. But they compound: each drain-reset cycle may trigger a
nonce persist (if the outgoing nonce crossed a batch boundary during
the cycle). 16 cycles * probability of nonce persist per cycle *
4-20ms = potential 64-320ms of compounded stalls.

The nonce persist decoupling solution (async fire-and-forget write
with in-memory monotonic counter) is correct. But it introduces a
new failure mode: if the system crashes during the async write
window, up to 256 nonces are lost. The design says "nonce gap (up
to 256 values) is lost, but monotonicity is preserved." This is
correct: the next startup reads the persisted value and starts from
there. But the gap means 256 potential frames were "consumed" from
the nonce space without being sent. At 256 nonces per batch and a
u64 nonce space, this is negligible. The gap is safe.

The interaction that nobody models: 16 drain-reset cycles * async
nonce persists * WiFi power-save period (500ms) = the event loop
spends 10-30% of each WiFi cycle on arena management and nonce
bookkeeping. On a Pi Zero, this may push CPU utilization above the
Active threshold, causing a metabolic state transition during the
WiFi burst. The metabolic state transition changes heartbeat
intervals, which changes how many heartbeats are generated during
the next WiFi burst, which changes the arena pressure. This is a
feedback loop between WiFi power management and the metabolic state
machine that nobody has modeled.

### Structural Hole 3: The Convergence-Replication Ordering Gap

Expert A identifies that filter reconciliation timing relative to
convergence fronts is unspecified. The devil's advocate identifies
that the Savant's Active/Stress halt creates a deadlock. Expert A
fixes the deadlock by setting minimum budget = 1.

Nobody addresses the ordering between filter reconciliation
completion and the replication decision. My most_under_replicated()
requires accurate replica counts. Replica counts come from the
content filter. The content filter is reconciled during/after
convergence. If the replication decision runs before filter
reconciliation is complete, the replica counts are stale.

The interaction: convergence Front 1 starts GSet merge. Filter
reconciliation starts concurrently (triggered by reconnection).
The replication work loop runs on every heartbeat tick. The first
heartbeat tick after reconnection evaluates most_under_replicated()
with pre-reconciliation filter data. The replication decision is
wrong. The peer may replicate matter that is already replicated
(the remote cluster has it but the filter has not reconciled yet)
or skip matter that needs replication (the remote cluster lost it
during the partition but the filter still shows the old state).

Expert A's probabilistic roll mitigates this: even with stale
filter data, the probability of initiating a transfer is bounded.
But my priority queue exacerbates it: the most_under_replicated()
ordering is deterministic, so all peers with the same stale filter
data make the same wrong decision simultaneously.

The fix: gate the replication work loop on filter reconciliation
completion. Replication should not run until both filter_root
values (from the reconnected cluster pair) match. This is a new
convergence gate predicate: REPLICATION_READY = filter_roots_match
AND convergence_front >= 2.

## Section 3: Novel Mechanisms

### Mechanism 1: Supersaturation-Weighted Replication Growth

**(a) Crystallographic description:**
Crystal growth rate at a face is proportional to the supersaturation
at that face. A face with high supersaturation (many dissolved atoms
nearby, few lattice sites occupied) grows faster than a face with low
supersaturation. The growth rate follows a sigmoid curve: below
critical supersaturation, no growth occurs (activation barrier).
Above critical supersaturation, growth accelerates. At extreme
supersaturation, growth plateaus (limited by diffusion rate).

**(b) Verkko concrete terms:**
Replace the flat replication budget with a supersaturation-weighted
growth rate:

```
fn replication_growth_rate(
    deficit: u8,          // target_replicas - current_replicas
    priority: MatterPriority, // P1..P4
    metabolic: MetabolicState,
) -> u8 {  // chunks per tick
    let saturation_q16 = (deficit as u32) << 16
        / (priority.target_replicas() as u32);
    // saturation_q16 is 0x10000 when deficit == target (0 replicas)
    // saturation_q16 is 0 when deficit == 0 (fully replicated)

    let base_rate = match metabolic {
        Background => 2,
        Idle => 5,
        Active => 3,    // NOT zero. NOT one. Scaled.
        Stress => 1,    // minimum, not zero.
    };

    // Scale base_rate by supersaturation
    let scaled = (base_rate as u32 * saturation_q16) >> 16;
    scaled.max(1) as u8
    // Minimum 1 chunk per tick in all states.
    // At full supersaturation (0 replicas): base_rate chunks.
    // At half supersaturation: base_rate/2 chunks.
}
```

Key properties:
- Never zero. Expert A's deadlock is avoided.
- Not flat. Expert A's budget = 1 in Active mode is replaced with
  a rate that scales with urgency. A P1 blob at 0 replicas in Active
  mode gets rate = 3. A P4 blob at 2/3 replicas in Active mode gets
  rate = 1. The system prioritizes by need, not by budget.
- Priority-aware. P1 blobs have target_replicas = 3 (presumably).
  P4 blobs have target_replicas = 1. The supersaturation calculation
  normalizes by target, so 1/3 missing on P1 has the same urgency
  as 1/1 missing on P4. This may not be correct; P1 should have
  higher absolute priority. The fix: multiply by a priority weight.

**(c) Emergent behavior from interaction:**
This mechanism interacts with Expert A's probabilistic roll and the
metabolic state machine. The probabilistic roll determines WHO
replicates. The supersaturation rate determines HOW FAST. The
metabolic state provides the base rate. The three mechanisms form
a replication triad:

- Priority queue: WHAT to replicate (most under-replicated first).
- Probabilistic roll: WHO replicates (BLAKE3 per-peer deduplication).
- Supersaturation rate: HOW FAST (scales with deficit, not flat).

The emergent behavior: after a partition heal, the most under-
replicated P1 blobs get the highest growth rate, distributed across
peers by the probabilistic roll, throttled by the metabolic state
but never halted. The replication front propagates through the mesh
like a crystallization wavefront: the most supersaturated regions
crystallize first, then the wave moves to less supersaturated
regions. This is the physical behavior of crystal growth and it is
the correct behavior for replication catch-up.

### Mechanism 2: The Diagnostic Path Triangle

**(a) Crystallographic description:**
In X-ray crystallography, you determine crystal structure by
comparing diffraction patterns from multiple angles. No single
angle gives the full structure. You need at least three linearly
independent diffraction vectors to reconstruct the unit cell.

**(b) Verkko concrete terms:**
Three health measurements form a diagnostic triangle for every
inter-cluster connection:

```
PathTriangle {
    direct_health: HealthScore,    // EMA of direct-path SACK ratios
    relay_health: HealthScore,     // EMA of relay-path SACK ratios
    wifi_quality: CapacityRatio,   // local link quality estimate

    fn diagnose(&self) -> PathDiagnosis {
        let d = self.direct_health.value_q16();
        let r = self.relay_health.value_q16();
        let w = self.wifi_quality.value_q16();

        // Threshold: 0x0000C000 = 0.75 in Q16.16
        let t = 0x0000C000u32;

        match (d >= t, r >= t, w >= t) {
            (true,  true,  true)  => PathDiagnosis::AllHealthy,
            (true,  false, true)  => PathDiagnosis::RelayFault,
            (false, true,  true)  => PathDiagnosis::NatExpired,
            (false, false, true)  => PathDiagnosis::DestinationFault,
            (true,  true,  false) => PathDiagnosis::WifiTransient,
            (true,  false, false) => PathDiagnosis::WifiPlusRelay,
            (false, true,  false) => PathDiagnosis::WifiPlusDirect,
            (false, false, false) => PathDiagnosis::LocalLinkDown,
        }
    }
}
```

Automated responses:
- RelayFault: failover to warm standby relay.
- NatExpired: send keepalive to renew NAT mapping.
- DestinationFault: no path change helps; scar the destination.
- WifiTransient: attenuate scar generation (see Mechanism 3).
- WifiPlusRelay: attenuate scars AND failover relay.
- WifiPlusDirect: attenuate scars AND renew NAT mapping.
- LocalLinkDown: halt outgoing traffic; enter degraded mode.

**(c) Emergent behavior from interaction:**
The diagnostic triangle interacts with the scar system, the relay
failover, and the metabolic governor. When the diagnosis is
WifiTransient, the scar generation threshold widens (Mechanism 3),
the metabolic governor applies WiFi-aware adjustments (devil's
advocate's proposal), and the relay path is preserved (no failover
triggered). The three responses are coordinated by the single
diagnosis, not by three independent mechanisms operating on the same
symptoms.

Without the triangle, the system sees "direct path degraded" and
scars the destination. With the triangle, the system sees "direct
path degraded AND wifi bad" and attributes the degradation to WiFi.
The scar goes to the link, not the peer. The dominance cascade is
clean.

### Mechanism 3: WiFi-Aware Scar Attenuation (The Defect Anneal)

**(a) Crystallographic description:**
In an annealing process, the temperature determines which defects
can move and which are frozen. At high temperature, vacancies
diffuse freely. At low temperature, vacancies are frozen in place.
The annealing schedule controls the final defect density.

WiFi interference is thermal noise in the lattice. It creates point
defects (missed heartbeats) that migrate into scars. The scar decay
rate (180 days) is the defect's thermal stability: a scar created at
high temperature (WiFi interference) should have low thermal
stability (short decay). A scar created at low temperature (genuine
Byzantine behavior) should have high thermal stability (180-day
decay).

**(b) Verkko concrete terms:**
Scars carry a source_attribution field. I propose extending the
ScarAttribution enum:

```
ScarAttribution {
    // Existing
    DirectObservation { observer, subject },
    CorrelatedSuspicion { observer, subject, confidence: u16 }, // Q8.8
    PathFault { path: PathId },
    // New
    WifiInduced { observer, wifi_quality_at_observation: u16 }, // Q8.8
}
```

Scar decay formula, extended for WiFi attribution:

```
fn scar_weight_q16(scar: &Scar, now: Epoch) -> u32 {
    let dt_hours = (now - scar.observation_epoch) as u64;
    let decay_days = match scar.attribution {
        WifiInduced { wifi_quality, .. } => {
            // Decay period: 1-7 days, inversely proportional
            // to WiFi quality at observation time.
            // wifi_quality 0.85 (Q8.8: 0x00D9) -> 3 days
            // wifi_quality 0.60 (Q8.8: 0x0099) -> 1 day
            // wifi_quality 0.95 (Q8.8: 0x00F3) -> 7 days
            let q = wifi_quality as u32;
            // days = 1 + (wifi_quality * 6) in Q8.8
            let days_q8_8 = 0x0100 + ((q * 6) >> 0);
            days_q8_8 >> 8  // truncate to integer days
        }
        _ => 180, // standard 180-day decay
    };

    let decay_hours = decay_days as u64 * 24;
    let decay_q16 = (dt_hours * 0x10000u64) / decay_hours;
    if decay_q16 >= 0x10000 { 0 }
    else { 0x10000u32 - decay_q16 as u32 }
}
```

The devil's advocate proposes a flat 24-hour decay for intra-cluster
WiFi scars. My mechanism is adaptive: worse WiFi at observation time
produces shorter decay. A scar from a 60% delivery ratio event
decays in 1 day. A scar from an 85% delivery ratio event decays in
3 days. A scar from a 95% delivery ratio "WiFi event" (borderline
normal) decays in 7 days.

The decision of whether a scar is WiFi-induced comes from the
diagnostic triangle. When PathDiagnosis is WifiTransient,
WifiPlusRelay, WifiPlusDirect, or LocalLinkDown, scars generated
during that diagnosis period are attributed as WifiInduced with the
current wifi_quality value. When PathDiagnosis is DestinationFault
or RelayFault, scars are attributed normally (DirectObservation or
PathFault).

**(c) Emergent behavior from interaction:**
WiFi-attributed scars interact with the dominance cascade. Because
WifiInduced scars decay in 1-7 days instead of 180, their impact
on the dominance score is transient. A peer in a WiFi-congested
apartment building accumulates WiFi scars daily, but yesterday's
scars decay before tomorrow's accumulate. The steady-state WiFi scar
weight is bounded by: (daily scar count) * (average scar weight at
age = 1 day). If a peer gets 4 WiFi scars per day with 3-day decay,
the steady-state weight is roughly 4 * 3 * 0.67 = ~8 scar-days. At
180-day decay, 4 scars per day produces 4 * 180 * 0.5 = 360
scar-days. The WiFi-aware attenuation reduces dominance pollution by
45x.

This interacts with gateway election. WiFi-induced scars no longer
disqualify a peer from gateway. The gateway election uses dominance
score, which uses weighted scar count. With 45x less scar pollution,
the gateway election reflects peer trustworthiness (enrollment age,
key integrity, genuine faults) rather than WiFi environment.

The WiFi scar attenuation also interacts with the convergence
annealing model. During convergence, the DAMPED health mode
attenuates new health observations. WiFi-attributed scars created
during convergence (when the mesh is in DAMPED mode AND WiFi is
noisy) get double attenuation: DAMPED mode reduces the EMA impact,
and WifiInduced attribution reduces the scar decay period. The two
attenuations are independent and multiplicative. A scar that would
have polluted the dominance cascade for 180 days instead affects it
for ~2 days with reduced weight. The convergence exit finds a clean
lattice rather than a scarred one.

### Mechanism 4: Convergence-Gated Replication Start

**(a) Crystallographic description:**
In epitaxial crystal growth, you cannot grow a new layer until the
substrate surface is prepared. Growing on an unprepared surface
produces misfit dislocations. The surface preparation is the gate.

**(b) Verkko concrete terms:**
Add a new convergence gate predicate:

```
REPLICATION_READY = filter_reconciliation_complete
                    AND convergence_front >= Front2
```

The replication work loop checks REPLICATION_READY before evaluating
most_under_replicated(). If the gate is not satisfied, the work loop
skips the tick. This prevents replication decisions based on stale
filter data.

filter_reconciliation_complete is defined as: the local filter's
Merkle root matches the remote cluster's advertised root for all
reconnected cluster pairs. This is detected by comparing the
filter_root from the latest heartbeat with the locally computed root.

```
fn is_filter_reconciled(cluster_pair: ClusterPairId) -> bool {
    let local_root = local_filter.merkle_root();
    let remote_root = latest_heartbeat[cluster_pair].filter_root;

```

**(c) Emergent behavior from interaction:**
This gate interacts with the supersaturation-weighted growth rate.
Before the gate opens, the supersaturation is unknown (stale filter

The replication work loop goes from dormant to full rate in one tick.

budget over 3 heartbeat ticks (6 seconds at Active, 15 seconds at

= 1/2. Tick 3: full rate. This 3-tick ramp prevents the CPU spike.

## Section 4: Response to the Devil's Advocate -- The WiFi Scar Cascade

The devil's advocate describes a cascade: WiFi interference (30
seconds) causes missed heartbeats, which cause false degradation
(3 seconds), which causes gateway election instability (5-10
seconds), which causes epoch divergence (10-15 seconds), which
causes permanent scars (180 days). The blast radius of a 30-second
WiFi event is 180 days of dominance pollution.

This cascade is real. My crystallographic lens reveals the structural
problem and a structural fix.

### The Structural Problem: Thermal Noise Entering the Permanent Record

In a crystal, thermal vibrations cause atoms to deviate from their
lattice positions. At low temperature, the deviations are small. At
high temperature, the deviations are large. If you record the atomic
positions during a heat spike and permanently store them, you have
contaminated your structure file with thermal noise. The correct
practice is to average over thermal fluctuations, recording only
positions that persist after cooling.

The scar system records positions during heat spikes. A 30-second
WiFi burst is a heat spike. The missed heartbeats are thermal
vibrations. The scar GSet records the vibrations permanently. 180
days later, the scar still contributes to the dominance cascade,
even though the WiFi burst ended 180 days ago.

The scar GSet is append-only. You cannot remove entries. The 180-day
read-time decay is the only mitigation, and 180 days is too long for
a thermal fluctuation.

### The Structural Fix: Three-Layer Defect Classification

The fix is not to change the scar decay period (the devil's advocate's
proposal) or to increase the scar threshold (also proposed). Those
are parameter tuning. The structural fix is to classify defects by
their thermal origin before recording them.

Layer 1: **Thermal defects (WiFi-induced).** These are fluctuations
caused by the environment, not by peer behavior. They are detected by
the diagnostic triangle (Mechanism 2). They are recorded as
WifiInduced scars with short adaptive decay (Mechanism 3). They
produce minimal dominance impact.

Layer 2: **Structural defects (genuine faults).** These are caused by
peer behavior: integrity failures, Byzantine actions, prolonged
disconnection beyond any WiFi plausibility window. They are recorded
as DirectObservation or CorrelatedSuspicion scars with 180-day decay.
They produce full dominance impact.

Layer 3: **Grain boundary defects (relay/path faults).** These are
caused by infrastructure between peers, not by the peers themselves.
They are recorded as PathFault scars with medium decay (30 days).
They produce moderate dominance impact on the relay, not on the
destination peer.

The three-layer classification is determined at scar creation time
by the diagnostic triangle's output:

```
fn classify_scar(diagnosis: PathDiagnosis) -> ScarLayer {
    match diagnosis {
        WifiTransient | WifiPlusRelay | WifiPlusDirect
            => ScarLayer::Thermal,
        RelayFault | WifiPlusRelay
            => ScarLayer::GrainBoundary,
        DestinationFault | AllHealthy
            => ScarLayer::Structural,
        LocalLinkDown
            => ScarLayer::Thermal, // local problem, not peer's fault
    }
}
```

Note: WifiPlusRelay maps to both Thermal and GrainBoundary. The scar
is recorded twice: once as WifiInduced (short decay, attenuate
dominance pollution) and once as PathFault (medium decay, track relay
reliability). Both are valid observations from different perspectives.

### Walking Through the Devil's Advocate's Cascade With the Fix

**Original cascade (devil's advocate):**

5-10s: epoch divergence. 10-15s: WiFi recovers, epoch reconciles.
15-30s: 180-day scars accumulate.

wifi_quality is low (delivery ratio 0.70 during interference burst).

NO scar generated. Local health degradation only (not gossiped,

5-10s: WiFi contention continues. 10 consecutive misses. Still

**What about a 60-second WiFi outage?**

60+s: WiFi recovers. The 5-day scar decays. By day 6, the dominance

### The Cascade That Remains: Genuine Catastrophic WiFi Failure

A 5-minute WiFi outage is not a thermal fluctuation. It is a
structural event: the WiFi link has failed. After 5 minutes (300
heartbeats missed), the system should respond:

1. 30 misses (30s): WifiInduced scar (5-day decay).
2. 60 misses (60s): second WifiInduced scar.
3. 120 misses (2 min): diagnostic triangle transitions to
   LocalLinkDown. Scar classification remains Thermal (local
   problem). But a new mechanism activates: the gateway isolates the
   non-responding peer from intra-cluster routing. Inter-cluster
   frames for dest_peer pointing to the isolated peer are buffered
   (not dropped) for up to 60 seconds, then dropped with a
   diagnostic event.
4. 300 misses (5 min): the peer is treated as departed. The peer's
   desk_peer value is marked as inactive (not retired; the peer may
   return). The gateway stops allocating resources to the peer's
   intra-cluster session. The managed arena reclaims the peer's 10KB

This graduated response is the correct crystallographic approach:
small thermal vibrations are absorbed. Large structural failures
produce graduated isolation. No 180-day scars from WiFi events.
No gateway thrashing from WiFi bursts. The dominance cascade
reflects peer behavior, not WiFi environment.

### The Devil's Advocate's Other Findings -- Brief Responses

**Arena budget during convergence:** The devil's advocate calculates
that convergence can consume most of the available arena headroom.
The hot arena overflow during WiFi bursts (500 frames buffered by
power management) is a real concern. The fix is not to resize the
hot arena (128 KB) but to cap the recvmmsg batch size dynamically
based on available arena space. If 50% of the hot arena is consumed,
halve the batch size. If 75% is consumed, process one frame at a
time. This adaptive batch sizing prevents overflow without increasing
the arena baseline.

**Cloud relay bootstrapping:** The devil's advocate correctly
identifies that a 2-peer mesh behind symmetric NATs is non-functional
without a cloud relay. This is a deployment constraint, not a
protocol design issue. The minimum fix (hardcode DHT bootstrap nodes)
is correct. The protocol does not need to change; the deployment
documentation needs to state that at least one peer or relay must
have a non-symmetric NAT or a cloud relay must be available.

**LEDBAT OWD measurement on WiFi:** The devil's advocate identifies
that WiFi power-save buffering adds up to 500ms of unmeasured delay
to LEDBAT's OWD measurement. The fix: the Conductor's wifi_quality
estimate feeds into the LEDBAT controller as a correction factor.
When wifi_quality < 0.90, the LEDBAT target widens by
(1 / wifi_quality^2). At 85% WiFi quality: target widens by ~1.38x.
This prevents LEDBAT from backing off due to WiFi buffering delay
while still responding to genuine WAN congestion.

**Nonce persist stall:** The devil's advocate identifies 4-20ms stalls
from synchronous nonce persistence during WiFi bursts. The async
nonce persist solution (NoncePersistRequest/NoncePersistComplete as
new event types in the Protocol trait) is correct. I add: the async
persist should use the SPSC ring buffer to the crypto thread, not a
separate I/O channel. The crypto thread already has flash access for
key persistence. Nonce persistence is the same operation class.
