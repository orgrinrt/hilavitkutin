## Section 1: The Three Load-Bearing Assumptions

After reading all four Stage 1 reviews and the tension map, I identify
three assumptions that every proposal depends on. These are not stated
as assumptions; they are taken as facts. That is what makes them
dangerous.

### Assumption 1: LAN Intra-Cluster Communication is Reliable and Fast

**Who depends on it:** Everyone. Expert A's intra-cluster DEK-based
Noise KK sessions assume LAN. The Savant's 1-second intra-cluster
heartbeat interval assumes LAN. Expert A's gateway handoff ("in-flight
frames may be lost during the handoff window; this is acceptable: the
heartbeat failure detection provides the recovery trigger") assumes
that heartbeat detection is fast enough that the window is small. The
Savant's simplified intra-cluster health scoring ("No dual-EMA. A
single heartbeat counter. 3 missed intra-cluster heartbeats = 3
seconds triggers a local-only health degradation") assumes that 3
seconds is the right detection time for LAN.

**Where the assumption is stated:**

- Savant, 4.1: "LAN latency is negligible; the variable interval is
  for WAN conservation."
- Expert A, Problem 2(d): "No polygon scheduling (LAN RTT is
  negligible)."
- Expert A, Problem 2(d): "Partition within a cluster... the gateway
  detects this via intra-cluster heartbeat timeout."

**Why it is fragile:**

The deployment target is household devices. Household devices connect
via WiFi. WiFi in 2026 is 802.11ax (WiFi 6) or 802.11be (WiFi 7) on
paper, but in practice most household deployments look like this:

- Router in the living room. Pi in the basement. NAS in the office.
  Phone on the couch.
- 2.4 GHz band congested by neighbors (8-15 networks visible in a
  typical apartment building).
- Microwave oven causes 50-500ms burst losses on 2.4 GHz every time
  someone heats coffee.
- Smart home devices (Zigbee, Thread) share 2.4 GHz and cause
  intermittent interference.
- WiFi power management puts interfaces to sleep. The Pi's WiFi
  adapter may have 100-500ms wake latency when the device is in
  power-save mode.

Real-world WiFi packet loss in a household: 1-15% under normal
conditions, 15-40% during interference bursts (microwave, baby
monitor, nearby microwave mesh backhaul). 99th percentile latency
on consumer WiFi: 50-200ms (not the 1ms LAN assumption). When two
devices are on different bands (one on 5 GHz, one on 2.4 GHz routed
through the AP), inter-device latency can spike to 500ms during
channel contention.

**Concrete failure numbers:**

At 15% packet loss (moderate household WiFi congestion), a 1-second
heartbeat interval means:
- P(miss 1) = 0.15
- P(miss 2 consecutive) = 0.15^2 = 0.0225
- P(miss 3 consecutive) = 0.15^3 = 0.0034

With a 3-missed threshold (3 seconds), false positives occur once per
294 intervals, which is once every 4.9 minutes. Every 5 minutes, the
gateway falsely concludes that an intra-cluster peer is degraded. This
triggers a scar. At 10 scars per day, the intra-cluster health is
permanently damaged.

At 30% packet loss (interference burst), P(miss 3) = 0.027. False
positive every 37 intervals = every 37 seconds. The cluster is in
permanent degradation during the interference burst. If the burst
lasts 2 minutes (a common microwave run), the gateway has declared
multiple scars and potentially triggered a gateway election cycle.

The Savant's simplified intra-cluster health scoring ("3 missed
heartbeats triggers a local-only health degradation, 10 consecutive
misses triggers a scar") is tuned for Ethernet reliability on a WiFi
network. This is the most fragile assumption in the entire design.

**What Expert A misses:** The DEK-based Noise KK session model is
correct for security, but the gateway handoff during WiFi contention
is not. Expert A says "in-flight frames may be lost during the handoff
window. This is acceptable." On WiFi, the handoff window is not
milliseconds; it is seconds. A gateway handoff triggered by a false
positive (WiFi interference) causes real disruption (CPSK session
teardown, inter-cluster frame loss) for a non-existent problem (the
"failed" peer was fine, just experiencing WiFi contention).

**What Expert B misses:** The sans-IO polling model assumes the
consumer calls handle() promptly after recvmmsg. On a Raspberry Pi
with WiFi power management, the recvmmsg may return with significant
jitter (50-200ms). If the event loop iteration takes 200ms due to
WiFi wake latency, timer deadlines are missed by 200ms. A heartbeat
interval of 1 second with 200ms jitter means the heartbeat is
effectively 800ms-1200ms. The 3-missed threshold fires after
2.4-3.6 seconds, not 3 seconds. The timer precision is worse than
Expert B's analysis of the timer wheel (1ms resolution) implies,
because the bottleneck is not the timer wheel but the WiFi adapter's
wake latency.

### Assumption 2: The Arena Memory Budget is Sufficient

**Who depends on it:** Expert B's entire polling model. The Savant's
resource accounting. Expert A implicitly (gateway routing within
cluster requires additional managed arena).

**Where the assumption is stated:**

- Expert B, Problem 1(d): "Memory per stream: ~32 * 24 bytes
  (UnackedFrame) + 48 bytes (StreamRetransmitState) = 816 bytes.
  At 11 connections * 10 active streams each = ~88 KB. Fits within
  the pool arena (256KB)."
- Savant, 4.1: "Resource accounting: intra-cluster sessions use a
  separate ThresholdGuard instance with a 10KB arena allocation per
  peer (60KB maximum for a 6-peer cluster). This is added to the
  managed arena baseline (200KB + 60KB = 260KB total)."
- Design: "hot (64KB bump), pool (256KB fixed-block), managed
  (~200KB system-budgeted)."

**Why it is fragile:**

The arena budget was computed for steady-state operation with 11
inter-cluster connections. Nobody has computed the budget for the
worst case: convergence after a long partition, with all connections
re-establishing simultaneously.

Convergence scenario: 12-cluster mesh partitions into two halves
(6 clusters each). Partition lasts 4 hours. On reconnection:

1. Each gateway re-establishes up to 6 inter-cluster Noise KK
   sessions simultaneously. Each handshake requires ~3 arena
   allocations (handshake message, response, application payload).
   At 1232 bytes per frame: 6 * 3 * 1232 = ~22 KB of hot arena for
   handshake frames alone.

2. Front 1 fires: GSet merge. Each side has 4 hours of control log
   entries to exchange. If each side accumulated 50 entries (enrollment
   changes, key rotations, scar entries), the flood gossip sends 50 *
   ~200 bytes = 10 KB per peer pair. At 6 peers: 60 KB.

3. Filter reconciliation starts concurrently: Merkle delta exchange.
   Each cluster's filter is ~128 KB (counting cuckoo filter). The
   delta could be up to 50% of the filter (2 hours of content changes
   on each side). Delta transmission: ~64 KB per cluster pair. At 6
   cluster pairs: 384 KB.

4. VitalQueue is draining KeyBundle operations (Front 2 consensus,
   fencing token resolution). These are vital-tier, competing with
   handshake frames for the reserved vital portion of the arena.

The hot arena (64KB) overflows during step 1 if the consumer does not
drain fast enough. The pool arena (256KB) overflows during step 2 if
retransmission buffers are needed for the gossip burst. The managed
arena (200KB + 60KB intra-cluster = 260KB) may be insufficient if
convergence requires tracking state for 6 new inter-cluster
connections plus 5 intra-cluster connections simultaneously.

**Concrete overflow scenario:**

Managed arena accounting during convergence:
- 11 inter-cluster connections * (~2-5KB per connection state) = 22-55 KB
- 5 intra-cluster connections * (~2KB per connection state) = 10 KB
- Per-stream retransmit state: 11 connections * 10 streams * 816 bytes
  = ~88 KB
- Convergence state: wavefront timer, gate predicates, per-cluster
  convergence state = ~4 KB
- Health pipeline: 11 dual-EMA tracks * ~64 bytes = ~0.7 KB
- Replay window: 11 connections * 128 bytes = ~1.4 KB
- SACK tracking: 11 connections * 10 streams * ~36 bytes = ~4 KB

Total managed arena during convergence: 130-163 KB. This fits in
260 KB, but with only 97-130 KB headroom. During convergence, the
filter reconciliation delta (up to 64 KB per cluster pair) must be
stored somewhere. If it goes in managed arena (it persists across
arena resets), headroom drops to 33-66 KB.

The pool arena (256 KB, fixed-block) holds retransmission buffers.
During convergence gossip burst, the vital and reactive tier streams
carry control log entries and KeyBundle operations. If 50 entries
per peer pair are in flight and 6 peer pairs are reconciling
simultaneously, the retransmission buffer holds up to 50 * 6 * 200
bytes = 60 KB of unacknowledged frames. On WiFi with 15% loss, these
frames need retransmission. Two retransmission attempts double the
buffer: 120 KB. Add steady-state retransmit buffers (88 KB) and the
pool arena is at 208 KB of 256 KB. One more burst and it overflows.

**Nobody computed the convergence burst budget.** Expert B computed
steady-state. The Savant added intra-cluster accounting. Expert A
specified the convergence protocol. Nobody multiplied them together.

The hot arena is the most vulnerable: 64 KB must hold all datagrams
in a recvmmsg batch (Expert B recommends capping at 32 datagrams =
~39 KB) plus all generated outputs. During convergence, each
incoming datagram can trigger multiple outputs (SACK + gossip forward
+ heartbeat response). If each input datagram generates 3 output
frames of 1232 bytes, 32 input datagrams produce 96 output frames =
~118 KB. The 64 KB hot arena overflows after processing the 17th
input datagram.

Expert B's calling convention handles ArenaExhausted by draining
outputs, resetting the arena, and retrying. This works for single
iterations. During convergence, the arena may exhaust on every
iteration, causing constant drain-reset-retry cycles. The latency
impact: each drain-reset adds one sendmmsg syscall (~10-50us on
ARM). At 32 iterations per batch: 320-1600us. The event loop
iteration time doubles or triples during convergence, at exactly
the moment when timely processing is most critical.

### Assumption 3: The Relay is a Household Device with Adequate Resources

**Who depends on it:** My own (Expert C) relay forwarding model.
Expert A's replication model ("Relay peers participate" in
replication). The Savant's relay pseudo-peer model.

**Where the assumption is stated:**

- Expert C, Section 1.5: "At 10,000 frames/second (a pathological
  case for a household relay): 3ms of CPU per second, ~0.3% of one
  core."
- Expert C, Section 1.8: "At 10 Mbps symmetric and 10 active relayed
  sessions: 500 kbps per session."
- Savant, 4.2: "The relay authenticates to the mesh via a relay-
  specific enrollment process."

**Why it is fragile:**

The deployment envelope says "household devices." The most common
household device that would serve as a relay is a Raspberry Pi. The
Raspberry Pi lineup in 2026:

| Device | RAM | CPU | WiFi | Cost |
|--------|-----|-----|------|------|
| Pi Zero 2W | 512 MB | Cortex-A53 1GHz quad | 802.11n 2.4GHz | ~$15 |
| Pi 4 | 1-8 GB | Cortex-A72 1.8GHz quad | 802.11ac dual-band | ~$35 |
| Pi 5 | 2-8 GB | Cortex-A76 2.4GHz quad | 802.11ac dual-band | ~$50 |

A Pi Zero 2W as a relay peer:
- 512 MB RAM total. Linux kernel + OS: ~128 MB. saalis binary +
  SQLite + hot store: ~64 MB. Available for relay: ~320 MB. But the
  Pi Zero is also running saalis as a mesh peer (it has its own
  content library). After the stash, content filter, managed arena,
  and hot store, available memory for relay operations might be
  50-100 MB. The forwarding table (25 KB) fits easily. The Noise
  session state per relayed endpoint (Expert C's model: relay is a
  full Noise KK participant): ~2 KB per session (Noise state +
  replay window + SACK tracking if the relay runs the full protocol
  state machine). At 256 forwarding entries: 512 KB. Fine for memory.

- CPU: Cortex-A53 at 1 GHz. ChaCha20 throughput on Cortex-A53
  (without NEON optimization): ~100 MB/s for ChaCha20-Poly1305.
  Per-frame AEAD (if the relay performs AEAD): 1232 bytes * ~10ns/byte
  = ~12us per frame. At 10,000 frames/second: 120ms of CPU per second
  = 12% of one core. But wait -- the relay does NOT perform AEAD.
  It forwards frames without decryption. The per-frame cost is ~300ns
  (hash lookup + memcpy). At 10,000 frames/second: 3ms/second. Fine.

  But this ignores the fact that the relay also runs the full Noise KK
  state machine for session establishment. During a convergence event
  where 6 clusters re-establish sessions through the relay, the relay
  processes 6 * 2 = 12 Noise handshakes. Each handshake involves 2
  Curve25519 DH operations. On Cortex-A53: ~3ms per DH (without
  hardware acceleration). 12 handshakes * 4 DH operations each = 48
  DH operations * 3ms = 144ms. During this 144ms, the relay is not
  forwarding frames for existing sessions. The event loop is blocked
  on Curve25519.

  Expert B's CryptoRequest/CryptoResult pattern would solve this by
  offloading DH to a crypto thread. But Expert B explicitly says his
  model assumes Ed25519 and bao verification are the expensive
  operations. Curve25519 DH is not mentioned. On a Pi Zero (4 cores
  at 1 GHz), the crypto thread consumes one core. The main event
  loop, the relay forwarding, and all other processing share three
  cores. During the 144ms burst, one core is saturated with DH
  operations while the other three handle everything else. On a
  single-core device (Pi Zero W, discontinued but still deployed):
  the 144ms blocks the entire system.

- WiFi throughput: The Pi Zero 2W has 802.11n 2.4 GHz. Real-world
  throughput: 15-50 Mbps (optimistic). With 50% allocated to relay
  forwarding (my own recommendation from Section 1.8): 7.5-25 Mbps.
  Per session: 750 kbps to 2.5 Mbps. Sufficient for heartbeats and
  metadata, but a single matter transfer at LEDBAT pace could
  consume the entire relay budget.

The fragility is not in any single resource dimension. It is in the
combination: during convergence, the relay simultaneously performs
multiple Noise handshakes (CPU spike), forwards burst gossip traffic
(bandwidth spike), and processes SIGNAL operations from re-registering
peers (forwarding table churn). Each dimension is individually
manageable; combined, they create a 100-500ms processing stall on a
Pi Zero 2W. During this stall, heartbeats are delayed, existing
sessions time out, and the mesh interprets the relay stall as a
relay failure, triggering relay failover. The relay failover causes
peers to re-register with a secondary relay, which generates more
SIGNAL traffic, which creates more load on the new relay. This is a
cascading failure triggered by convergence.

## Section 2: The Most Fragile Assumption -- Failure Cascade Analysis

**Assumption 1 (LAN reliability) is the most fragile.** It is the only
assumption that fails routinely in normal operation (not just during
convergence or device failure). WiFi interference is constant. Every
household has a microwave. Every apartment building has WiFi
congestion.

### What breaks first

**Intra-cluster health scoring fires false positives.**

The Savant's simplified health scoring (3 missed 1-second heartbeats
= degradation, 10 consecutive misses = scar) is the first casualty.
At 15% WiFi packet loss:

- False degradation event every 4.9 minutes.
- False scar every ~3.3 hours (10 consecutive misses at 15% loss:
  P(10 consecutive) = 0.15^10 = 5.7e-9 per interval. This is
  actually extremely unlikely at 15%. Let me recalculate: at 15%
  loss, 10 consecutive misses: 0.15^10 is vanishingly small.
  The real problem is burst losses.)

WiFi burst losses are the threat. A 5-second WiFi blackout (AP
channel switch, power management, DFS radar event) causes 5
consecutive missed heartbeats. At the Savant's thresholds, 5 misses
is past the degradation trigger (3) but below the scar trigger (10).
The gateway marks the peer as degraded. When the WiFi recovers, the
peer resumes heartbeats, but the recovery is slow (the Savant says
"no dual-EMA, a single heartbeat counter"). How fast does recovery
happen? Unspecified. If recovery requires 10 consecutive successful
heartbeats (symmetric with degradation), recovery takes 10 seconds.
During those 10 seconds, the peer is marked as degraded even though
it is fully operational.

A 15-second WiFi blackout (ISP modem reboot, WiFi driver crash, AP
firmware update) causes 15 consecutive missed heartbeats. This
triggers both degradation (3 misses) and a scar (10 misses). The
scar is permanent (GSet, append-only). The peer now has a scar that
affects its dominance score for 180 days. A WiFi glitch that lasted
15 seconds has a 180-day impact on the peer's standing in the mesh.

### What breaks next

**Gateway election instability.**

If the gateway peer experiences a WiFi blackout while a non-gateway
peer does not (they are on different bands, or different WiFi
adapter models), the non-gateway peer may see the gateway as
degraded. The max-register CRDT for gateway election uses
availability as the primary criterion. If the gateway's intra-cluster
health is degraded, the non-gateway peer may propose itself as the
new gateway.

The gateway election is max-register (merge = max). The proposal
propagates within the cluster. If the WiFi recovers before the
proposal propagates to all peers (possible on LAN, where propagation
is also subject to the same WiFi losses), the cluster has two peers
that believe different gateway states:

- Peer A (the current gateway, recovered from WiFi blackout): thinks
  it is still the gateway.
- Peer B (proposed new gateway): thinks it is the gateway.
- Peers C-F: may have received Peer B's proposal, or may not have.

The max-register CRDT will eventually resolve this (convergent by
construction). But during the resolution window, both Peer A and
Peer B are acting as gateways. Both establish inter-cluster sessions.
Both respond to intra-cluster routing requests. The remote clusters
see two gateways for the same cluster and may establish sessions
with both. The design says "per-connected-component gateway
singularity (Invariant 2)" but the connected component is
unreliable (WiFi), so the gateway singularity invariant is violated
for the duration of the WiFi contention event.

**Duration of violation:** The max-register merge happens on the
next successful intra-cluster heartbeat exchange. At 1-second
intervals and 15% packet loss, the expected time to a successful
exchange is ~1.2 seconds (geometric distribution with p=0.85). But
during WiFi contention, the loss rate is higher. At 40% loss during
a burst, expected time to successful exchange is ~1.7 seconds.

During these 1-2 seconds, two gateways are active. Two sets of
inter-cluster sessions exist. CPSK state is being managed by two
peers. Key material may be distributed by both. This is not a
theoretical concern; it is the normal operating condition of a WiFi
household mesh.

### Blast radius

**Convergence triggered by a WiFi microwave event.**

The worst case: a 30-second WiFi interference burst (caused by a
baby monitor, a neighbor's WiFi mesh backhaul, or a combined
microwave + video call bandwidth spike) causes the following cascade:

1. **0-3 seconds:** Intra-cluster heartbeats fail. Gateway marks
   peer as degraded. (Local only.)

2. **3-5 seconds:** Gateway election instability. Two gateways
   emerge briefly. Remote clusters see two CPSK proposals.

3. **5-10 seconds:** The dual-gateway state causes conflicting CPSK
   epoch advances. Each "gateway" renegotiates its own CPSK sessions.
   The epoch counters diverge.

4. **10-15 seconds:** WiFi recovers. Max-register resolves gateway
   to one peer. But epoch counters have diverged. The HKDF fast-
   forward protocol must reconcile.

5. **15-30 seconds:** The HKDF fast-forward iterates up to 720
   steps. On a Pi Zero at ~3us per HKDF step: 2.2ms. Negligible.
   But during fast-forward, frames encrypted under the wrong epoch
   are dropped. Any matter transfer in progress is interrupted.

6. **30+ seconds:** If the WiFi interference continues or recurs,
   the cycle repeats. Each cycle produces a new scar. After 4 cycles
   in one day (not unreasonable in a congested apartment building),
   the peer has 4 scars. Each scar starts at weight 1.0 and decays
   over 180 days. The peer's dominance score is permanently suppressed
   by WiFi environmental factors, not by any actual misbehavior.

The blast radius of a WiFi interference event is: temporary gateway
instability (seconds), potential epoch divergence (seconds of
dropped frames), permanent scar accumulation (180-day impact on
dominance), and interrupted matter transfers (minutes of wasted
work). For a system designed for household use, this blast radius
is disproportionate to the trigger.

### Minimum Change to Survive This Assumption Failing

The intra-cluster heartbeat and health scoring must be designed for
WiFi, not Ethernet. Specific changes:

1. **Increase the scar threshold from 10 to 30 consecutive misses
   for intra-cluster.** At 1-second heartbeat intervals, 30
   consecutive misses = 30 seconds. A 30-second WiFi blackout is a
   real event (not interference). Shorter blackouts (microwave,
   channel switch) are 1-10 seconds and would not trigger scars. The
   false scar rate at 15% loss drops from once per 3.3 hours
   (impossible with 10 threshold) to zero (0.15^30 is astronomically
   small, and even burst losses rarely last 30 seconds).

2. **Add WiFi-aware heartbeat jitter tolerance.** Instead of "3
   missed heartbeats = degradation," use "3 missed heartbeats within
   a 10-second sliding window." This means: if 3 out of 10 heartbeats
   are missed, the peer is degraded. If 1-2 are missed (normal WiFi
   loss), the peer is fine. The sliding window absorbs the burst loss
   pattern of WiFi.

3. **Gateway election dampening for intra-cluster.** Do not trigger
   gateway election on intra-cluster health degradation alone. Require
   sustained degradation (10+ seconds, not 3 seconds) or explicit
   peer departure (enrollment revocation, clean shutdown) before
   considering a gateway change. This prevents WiFi-induced gateway
   thrashing.

4. **Separate intra-cluster scar tier.** Intra-cluster WiFi-induced
   scars should have a shorter decay than 180 days. A 24-hour decay
   for intra-cluster scars would be appropriate: WiFi conditions
   change day to day (neighbor moves, new device added, furniture
   rearranged), so a scar from yesterday's interference should not
   affect today's dominance score.

These changes are not large. They require adding a configuration
parameter (intra_cluster_scar_threshold: u8, default 30), modifying
the health counter to a sliding window (replace u8 counter with a
u32 bitmask of the last 32 heartbeat results), and adding a
dampening timer to gateway election. Total implementation impact:
~100 lines of state machine code. Total specification impact: ~30
lines of body text in the intra-cluster communication section.

## Section 3: The Five Tensions Under Real-World Deployment

I now attack each of the five tensions from the tension map under
three specific deployment scenarios:

- **Scenario W:** Household WiFi dropping 15% of packets (routine).
- **Scenario P:** Relay peer is a Raspberry Pi Zero 2W with 512 MB
  RAM and 802.11n WiFi.
- **Scenario M:** Mesh has exactly 2 peers (minimum viable deployment:
  one Pi running saalis, one phone browsing the web UI).

### Tension 1: SansIO Polling Convention (4-Method vs 2-Phase)

**Expert B proposes:** Four-method Protocol trait (handle, poll_transmit,
poll_event, poll_timeout) with arena parameter, modeled on quinn.

**Savant proposes:** Two-phase feed/poll with single poll_output(),
modeled on simpler state machines.

**Under Scenario W (15% WiFi packet loss):**

The polling convention determines how quickly the consumer can react to
bursty, lossy input. With 15% loss, the consumer receives 85% of
expected datagrams per recvmmsg batch. The missing 15% will trigger SACK
retransmissions on the sender side. The consumer's event loop must
handle the retransmitted frames (which arrive later) interleaved with
new frames from other connections.

Expert B's four-method split separates transmit drain from event drain.
This matters under packet loss because the consumer must send SACKs
(poll_transmit) before processing the application-level consequences
of the loss (poll_event). If SACKs are delayed because the consumer
is processing events first (Savant's single poll_output mixes them),
the sender's retransmission timer fires unnecessarily, creating
redundant retransmissions. On a Pi Zero's constrained WiFi, each
unnecessary retransmission consumes ~1232 bytes of the 15-50 Mbps
budget.

Quantitatively: at 1000 frames/second per connection and 11 connections,
the consumer processes ~11,000 frames/second. With 15% loss and a
1-second SACK interval, each connection generates ~1 SACK per second.
If SACK transmission is delayed by 5ms (because the consumer is
processing application events before reaching the SACK in the Savant's
mixed output queue), the sender may fire a spurious retransmission for
~150 frames (1000 * 0.15). On 11 connections: 1650 spurious
retransmissions per second, at 1232 bytes each = ~2 MB/s of wasted
bandwidth. On the Pi Zero's 15-50 Mbps WiFi: 0.3-1.1 Mbps wasted.

This argues for Expert B's four-method split, where poll_transmit
drains SACKs before poll_event processes application logic.

**Under Scenario P (Pi Zero 2W, 512 MB RAM):**

The arena model determines memory pressure. Expert B's ArenaRef<'a>
with explicit arena.reset() gives the consumer a single, clear
invalidation point. The Savant's "valid until the next feed()" rule
means the consumer must process all outputs between feed calls. On a
Pi Zero with slow WiFi, the time between feed calls may be long (WiFi
wake latency: 100-500ms). During this time, the arena holds the
previous iteration's output slices. If the consumer holds references
to arena data across multiple feed iterations (for batched sendmmsg),
the "valid until next feed()" rule prevents this.

Expert B's model is more flexible here: the consumer can hold arena
references until the explicit arena.reset(), which happens once per
event loop iteration (after all polling is complete). This allows
batched sendmmsg with multiple Transmit slices from the arena.

**Under Scenario M (2 peers, minimum viable):**

With exactly 2 peers, there is 1 connection. The polling convention
overhead is negligible. Both Expert B's four-method and the Savant's
two-phase work fine. The complexity of Expert B's four-method split
is unnecessary for 1 connection. But the design must handle 1-12
clusters, so optimizing for the minimum case is wrong. The 2-peer
scenario is a sanity check, not a design target.

**Verdict on Tension 1:** Expert B's four-method split wins under
WiFi conditions because it prioritizes SACK transmission over event
processing. But both experts missed the real problem: the event loop
iteration time on a Pi Zero with WiFi power management. Neither
polling convention matters if the event loop itself stalls for 200ms
waiting for WiFi wake. The polling convention should specify a
maximum acceptable event loop iteration time (e.g., 10ms) and
degrade gracefully when hardware cannot meet it.

### Tension 2: Relay-First vs Hole-Punch-First

**My Stage 1 position:** Relay-first, upgrade to direct.

**The design's stated invariant:** Hole-punch-first, relay as last
resort.

**Under Scenario W (15% WiFi packet loss):**

WiFi packet loss affects hole punching and relay equally for
intra-household traffic. But for inter-household traffic (WAN), the
question is different: the relay peer is inside the household (another
Pi or NAS), and the relay path goes Pi -> relay -> WAN -> remote.
The first hop (Pi -> relay) has 15% WiFi loss. The relay path has
this WiFi loss on both the source-to-relay and relay-to-source
legs, compounding to ~28% effective loss for the relayed path.

With hole-punch-first: the Pi attempts direct WAN hole punching
(bypassing the local relay). If hole punching succeeds, the path is
Pi -> WAN -> remote, with WiFi loss only on the first hop (15%
effective loss). This is better than the relay path (28% effective
loss). Hole-punch-first is correct for this scenario because it
avoids the local WiFi leg through the relay.

This is a counter-argument to my own relay-first recommendation.
When the relay is on the same WiFi network as the source, relay-first
doubles the WiFi loss. The relay-first model assumes the relay is
well-connected; on a household WiFi network, the relay suffers the
same WiFi problems as everyone else.

However, hole-punch-first fails for symmetric NATs (40% of
deployments). For those peers, relay is the only option. The
relay's WiFi leg is unavoidable.

**Revised position:** Relay-first is correct only if the relay is on
a wired connection or a different, less congested WiFi band. If the
relay is on the same WiFi band as the source, the relay path is worse
than direct. The design should classify relay quality by transport
type (wired relay vs WiFi relay) and prefer wired relays. This is
not in any expert's proposal.

**Under Scenario P (Pi Zero 2W as relay):**

A Pi Zero 2W as a relay with 802.11n 2.4 GHz WiFi:
- Real-world throughput: 15-30 Mbps (half-duplex, shared with all
  other devices on 2.4 GHz).
- Relay forwarding budget: 50% = 7.5-15 Mbps.
- Per-session bandwidth: 750 kbps to 1.5 Mbps (at 10 sessions).

This is insufficient for matter transfers. A 2 GB ROM file at 750
kbps takes 5.9 hours to transfer through the Pi Zero relay. If the
peer behind the symmetric NAT needs this file, it waits 6 hours.
With hole-punch-first, the transfer never goes through the relay
(if hole punching succeeds). With relay-first, the transfer starts
immediately through the relay at 750 kbps, then migrates to a direct
path (if hole punching succeeds in the background).

The relay-first approach wastes the first 2-10 seconds of transfer
bandwidth on the slow relay path before the direct path is available.
For a 2 GB transfer, wasting 10 seconds of 750 kbps relay bandwidth
is ~900 KB, negligible. Relay-first is correct for large transfers
because the migration from relay to direct is fast relative to the
total transfer time.

For heartbeats and metadata (small, frequent), relay-first is clearly
correct: the 500ms relay-first connectivity time is worth more than
the marginally higher bandwidth of a direct path that takes 2-10
seconds to establish.

**Under Scenario M (2 peers):**

With exactly 2 peers, if both are on the same LAN, no relay is
needed. If they are on different LANs, one relay is needed. The
relay must be a third-party device or one of the two peers must
relay for itself (which is a no-op). This scenario reveals a
fundamental problem: with 2 peers on different LANs and symmetric
NATs on both sides, there is no relay. Neither peer can reach the
other, and there is no third device to relay through.

The design assumes "2-12 clusters." With 2 clusters of 1 peer each,
if both are behind symmetric NATs, the mesh is non-functional. The
minimum viable deployment requires either: (a) at least one peer
without symmetric NAT, (b) a dedicated relay (cloud server or VPS),
or (c) a third peer that can reach both.

None of the four experts address this. Expert C (me) assumed "full
mesh peers, relay-enabled by default for gateways." But in a 2-peer
mesh, both peers are gateways (each cluster has 1 peer). If both
are behind symmetric NATs, neither can reach the other to become a
relay.

**This is a deployment killer.** The design must either: (a) require
a cloud relay for the minimum viable case, (b) require at least one
peer with a non-symmetric NAT, or (c) document that 2-peer meshes
across symmetric NATs are unsupported. Option (a) contradicts the
design's self-hosted ethos. Option (b) is an enrollment-time check
that no expert specified. Option (c) is honest but limits adoption.

### Tension 3: Gateway Handoff -- Teardown vs Migration

**Expert A proposes:** Teardown and re-establish intra-cluster sessions.
"In-flight frames may be lost. Heartbeat failure detection provides
recovery."

**Savant proposes:** Connection_id preserved across migration (relay
model extended to intra-cluster).

**Under Scenario W (15% WiFi packet loss):**

Gateway handoff on WiFi is already problematic (see Section 2). The
teardown model (Expert A) is worse under WiFi conditions because:

1. Teardown requires 6 new Noise KK handshakes (one per peer pair in
   a 6-peer cluster). Each handshake is 2 round trips. On WiFi with
   15% loss and 50ms median RTT: expected handshake completion time =
   2 * 50ms / 0.85 = ~118ms per handshake. At 6 handshakes: ~700ms
   total. During this 700ms, no intra-cluster communication is
   possible. Inter-cluster frames addressed to cluster peers via
   dest_peer routing are dropped.

2. The migration model (Savant) avoids the 700ms blackout because
   the connection_id is preserved. The new gateway takes ownership
   of existing session state. The transition time is bounded by one
   PATH_CHALLENGE/PATH_RESPONSE round trip: ~50ms on LAN (ignoring
   WiFi jitter).

On WiFi, the migration model is ~14x faster than the teardown model.
But the migration model requires the old gateway to transfer session
state to the new gateway before dying. If the gateway handoff was
triggered by the old gateway failing (WiFi blackout), the state
transfer cannot happen. The migration model requires a cooperative
handoff (old gateway is still alive and reachable). The teardown
model works even if the old gateway is dead.

**Under Scenario W, the correct approach is hybrid:** use migration
for cooperative handoffs (planned gateway changes, voluntary
shutdown) and teardown for non-cooperative handoffs (gateway
failure). This is what TCP does: orderly close (FIN) vs disorderly
close (RST). Neither Expert A nor the Savant proposes this hybrid.

**Under Scenario P (Pi Zero 2W):**

Gateway handoff on a Pi Zero: 6 Noise KK handshakes require 12
Curve25519 DH operations. On Cortex-A53 at 1 GHz: ~3ms per DH. Total
DH time: 36ms. Plus network round trips: 6 * 2 * 50ms / 0.85 =
~700ms. Total handoff time: ~736ms. During this time, the Pi Zero's
event loop is spending 36ms on DH (blocking if inline, non-blocking
if crypto thread). On a Pi Zero with 4 cores, the crypto thread
handles DH in 36ms while the event loop continues. The event loop
still cannot route inter-cluster frames to cluster peers for 700ms.

The migration model on a Pi Zero: 1 PATH_CHALLENGE round trip (~50ms)
plus state transfer (~2-5 KB of session state at ~10 MB/s WiFi
throughput = 0.5ms). Total: ~50ms. 14x faster.

**Under Scenario M (2 peers):**

With 2 peers, each cluster has 1 peer. Gateway handoff is
meaningless (the only peer is always the gateway). Tension 3 does
not apply.

### Tension 4: Replication -- Probabilistic vs Priority-Queue

**Expert A proposes:** Probabilistic deduplication with BLAKE3-based
per-peer roll.

**Savant proposes:** Priority queue (most_under_replicated first),
no explicit thundering herd mitigation.

**Under Scenario W (15% WiFi packet loss):**

Packet loss affects replication transfers. A 2 GB matter transfer
through WiFi with 15% loss at the link layer: the transport (LEDBAT
over reliable streams) handles retransmission, but the effective
throughput drops. On a 50 Mbps WiFi link with 15% loss: effective
throughput is approximately 50 * 0.85 * (1 - overhead_from_retransmit)
= ~35-40 Mbps. This is still fast enough for LAN replication.

The thundering herd problem is worse under packet loss because the
content filter reconciliation may produce inconsistent views during
loss bursts. If peer A's heartbeat (carrying the Merkle root) is
dropped, peer B does not learn about A's filter changes until the
next heartbeat. During this window, B's replication evaluation
uses stale filter data. B may conclude that matter X is under-
replicated (because B does not see A's filter advertising X) and
initiate a redundant transfer.

Expert A's probabilistic approach handles this correctly: the BLAKE3
roll is independent of filter freshness. Each peer makes a
probabilistic decision regardless of how fresh its filter view is.
The expected number of redundant transfers is bounded by the
probability formula.

The Savant's priority-queue approach is worse under packet loss
because it relies on accurate replica counts (from filters). Stale
filter data causes inaccurate replica counts, which causes incorrect
most_under_replicated ordering, which causes suboptimal replication
targets. On WiFi, filter data is always somewhat stale (1-2 heartbeat
intervals behind, more during loss bursts).

**Under Scenario P (Pi Zero 2W):**

The replication work loop runs on every heartbeat tick. On a Pi Zero,
the CPU budget for replication evaluation is tight. Expert A's
probabilistic approach: one BLAKE3 hash per blob per evaluation.
BLAKE3 on Cortex-A53: ~500 MB/s. Hash input: ~100 bytes (blob_id +
peer DK + epoch). Time per hash: ~200ns. At 10,000 blobs in the
stash: 2ms per evaluation. Acceptable.

The Savant's priority queue: most_under_replicated() requires
sorting all under-replicated matter by replica count. At 10,000
blobs, this is an O(n log n) operation. On Cortex-A53: ~133us per
comparison (with filter lookups). Total: 10,000 * 14 * 133us =
~18.6ms. This is borderline for a heartbeat tick evaluation. If the
heartbeat interval is 5 seconds (Idle mode), 18.6ms is 0.37% of the
interval. Fine. But if the heartbeat interval is 2 seconds (Active
mode), 18.6ms is 0.93%. Still fine, but the constant grows with
stash size.

The real problem: the Savant says "in Active/Stress mode:
replication_budget = 0." On a Pi Zero, the system is more likely to
be in Active or Stress mode (limited CPU and bandwidth push the
metabolic state upward). If the Pi Zero is in Active mode 50% of the
time, replication runs only during the other 50% (Idle/Background).
Combined with WiFi packet loss degrading transfer throughput,
replication on a Pi Zero may never catch up.

Expert A's approach does not halt replication based on metabolic
state (she uses the Conductor for "replication aggressiveness," not
an on/off switch). This is more robust on constrained devices.

**Under Scenario M (2 peers):**

With 2 peers, replication is simple: each piece of matter should
exist on both peers (target_replicas = 2). Expert A's probabilistic
approach: with 1 known holder and target 2, probability = 1/2. One
of the two peers initiates the transfer. Expected redundant transfers:
0.5 (only one peer initiates).

The Savant's priority queue: most_under_replicated() finds the same
matter on both peers. Both initiate transfer simultaneously. 100%
redundancy. This is the thundering herd for the 2-peer case, and the
Savant does not address it.

Expert A's approach is correct for the 2-peer case. The Savant's is
not.

**The Savant's Active/Stress halt creates a deadlock in the 2-peer
case:** After a partition between the 2 peers, convergence forces
Active/Stress. The Savant halts replication in Active/Stress. But the
only way to restore the 2-peer mesh's replication targets (each piece
of matter needs 2 copies) is to replicate. Convergence cannot exit
to Idle/Background until the system has recovered. The system cannot
recover without replication. Deadlock.

Expert A explicitly notes this: "The Savant's rule halts replication
entirely during Active/Stress, which means a partition heal produces
zero replication while the system is most in need of filling gaps."
The tension map calls this out as a potential deadlock. The Savant
must address this.

### Tension 5: Relay Trust Model -- Full Peer vs Pseudo-Peer

**Expert C (me) proposes:** Full Noise KK peer with DK/DEK, relay
as full protocol state machine participant.

**Savant proposes:** Pseudo-peer with DK/DEK but limited capabilities
(no gossip, no dominance, no health scoring).

**Under Scenario W (15% WiFi packet loss):**

The 30-second amplification prevention rule (my own recommendation)
creates a liveness hazard. In Background mode, heartbeats are sent
every 30 seconds. On WiFi with 15% loss, P(heartbeat lost) = 0.15.
If the heartbeat is lost, the relay has not received an authenticated
frame from that endpoint within 30 seconds. The next frame from the
other endpoint is dropped by the relay (amplification prevention).

The other endpoint's heartbeat arrives (assuming it was not also
lost). The relay now has a recent authenticated frame from endpoint B
but not from endpoint A. Traffic from B to A flows, but traffic from
A to B is blocked. One-directional flow persists until A's next
heartbeat succeeds.

Expected duration of one-directional flow: one heartbeat interval
(30 seconds in Background mode). P(occurrence per Background
heartbeat) = 0.15. Frequency: every ~200 seconds (30/0.15). In
Background mode, the relay drops traffic from one direction every
3.3 minutes on average. This is not a security feature; it is a
liveness bug disguised as a security feature.

**The fix:** Set the amplification prevention window to 2x the
heartbeat interval (60 seconds in Background mode, dynamically
adjusted by metabolic state). This adds 30 seconds of "stale but
authenticated" margin, absorbing one missed heartbeat. At 15%
loss, the probability of two consecutive missed heartbeats is 2.25%.
The one-directional flow event drops to once per 22 minutes. Still
not great, but an order of magnitude better.

Alternatively, the relay can use its own Noise session state to
validate that the endpoint was recently active, rather than requiring
a recent incoming frame. The Noise session has a sequence number; if
the sequence number advanced recently (even if the relay received the
frame on a different path), the endpoint is considered active.

**Under Scenario P (Pi Zero 2W as relay):**

If the relay runs the full protocol state machine (Expert C's model),
it runs the same code as any other peer. The memory budget: 11
inter-cluster connections * ~5 KB = 55 KB of connection state. Plus
256 forwarding entries * ~100 bytes = 25 KB. Plus managed arena
baseline = 200 KB. Total: ~280 KB. On a Pi Zero with 320 MB
available: fine.

If the relay is a pseudo-peer (Savant's model), it runs a simpler
forwarding loop. Memory: forwarding table (25 KB) + Noise session
state for each connected peer (~2 KB * 24 peers max) = ~73 KB.
Significantly less. The CPU savings are marginal (the relay does not
decrypt, regardless of model).

The pseudo-peer model uses ~4x less memory. On a Pi Zero, this
matters: the saved 207 KB (280 - 73) is available for stash,
content filter, or other operations.

But the pseudo-peer model has a significant complexity cost: a
separate relay runtime must be specified (Expert B's Protocol trait
does not apply). This is a separate codebase path, a separate testing
surface, and a separate specification document. The full-peer model
reuses the existing Protocol trait implementation.

**Under Scenario M (2 peers):**

With 2 peers behind symmetric NATs, there is no relay (as established
in Tension 2). The relay trust model is irrelevant. This reinforces
the finding that the minimum viable mesh requires either a third
device, a cloud relay, or non-symmetric NATs.

## Section 4: What Every Expert Missed

### Expert A missed: WiFi-induced scar accumulation

Expert A's scar system has a 180-day decay. Expert A's analysis
considers scars from real events (integrity failures, Byzantine
behavior, peer crashes). Expert A does not consider scars from WiFi
environmental factors. On a household WiFi network, WiFi-induced
scars will accumulate faster than they decay. The dominance cascade
is a pure function of GSet state; if the GSet is polluted with WiFi-
induced scars, the dominance ordering reflects WiFi quality, not peer
trustworthiness.

The design says "ScarAttribution has PathFault { path: PathId } which
could represent a relay path." But there is no PathFault attribution
for "intra-cluster WiFi interference." The scar system attributes
missed heartbeats to the peer, not to the WiFi link. This is
incorrect for a household deployment.

Expert A should add: WiFi-path-attributed scars with a shorter decay
(24 hours, not 180 days), or exclude intra-cluster heartbeat misses
from scar generation entirely (treat them as local health data only,
not as GSet-persisted scars).

### Expert B missed: recvmmsg batch size interaction with WiFi power management

Expert B recommends capping recvmmsg batch size at 32 datagrams. On
WiFi with power management, the WiFi adapter buffers frames while in
power-save mode and delivers them in a burst when waking. A 500ms
power-save interval at 1000 frames/second across all connections
produces 500 buffered frames. recvmmsg returns up to 32 of them. The
remaining 468 frames are returned in subsequent recvmmsg calls. But
the hot arena (64 KB) was sized for 32 datagrams. The event loop
must iterate 16 times to drain 500 buffered frames. Each iteration
processes 32 frames, drains outputs, and resets the arena. 16
iterations * ~10-50us per iteration = 160-800us.

This is not a problem in itself, but it interacts with timer
precision. If the event loop takes 800us to drain the WiFi burst,
timer deadlines that should have fired during those 800us are delayed.
For heartbeat timers (1-30 second intervals), 800us is negligible.
For SACK retransmission timers (1 second minimum), 800us is
negligible. For LEDBAT measurements (sub-millisecond), 800us is a
significant measurement error.

Expert B's analysis of LEDBAT says "LEDBAT timing should use kernel
timestamps (SO_TIMESTAMPING) directly, not the timer wheel." This is
correct but incomplete: SO_TIMESTAMPING reports the kernel's
timestamp, not the WiFi adapter's. On WiFi, the kernel timestamp
reflects when the frame reached the kernel, not when it was
transmitted by the remote peer. The WiFi power-save buffering adds
up to 500ms of unmeasured delay. LEDBAT's OWD measurement is
incorrect by up to 500ms on WiFi.

This means LEDBAT's target delay (10-100ms, depending on metabolic
state) is compared against measurements that include up to 500ms of
WiFi power-save buffering. LEDBAT will consistently measure high OWD
and back off, even when the WAN path is uncongested. The result:
matter transfers on WiFi run at the minimum LEDBAT rate, not the
available bandwidth.

Expert B should specify: disable WiFi power management on mesh peers
(a system-level configuration), or add a power-management-aware OWD
correction to the LEDBAT controller. The former is outside verkko's
scope (it is an OS setting). The latter requires detecting WiFi
power-management bursts (cluster of frames with similar kernel
timestamps followed by a gap) and adjusting OWD measurements
accordingly.

### Expert C (me) missed: the cloud relay bootstrapping problem

I specified relay selection, relay registration, relay failover, and
relay forwarding. I did not address: how does the mesh bootstrap when
no household device has a non-symmetric NAT? The answer is: it does
not. The design has no provision for a cloud relay or rendezvous
server.

Tailscale solves this with DERP servers (cloud-hosted relays).
WireGuard solves this with endpoint configuration (users manually
configure peer addresses). libp2p solves this with public bootstrap
nodes.

Verkko has none of these. The PeerDiscovery fallback chain ends at
"DHT (Mainline)." But Mainline DHT itself requires bootstrapping (the
peer must know at least one DHT node). The invite token contains
address_hints and relay_dht_key, but if the inviter is behind a
symmetric NAT, the address_hints are unreachable and the
relay_dht_key may point to a DHT entry that the invitee cannot
reach (because the invitee is also behind a symmetric NAT and cannot
reach the DHT bootstrap nodes without a relay, creating a circular
dependency).

The minimum fix: hardcode a set of public Mainline DHT bootstrap
nodes (standard practice for DHT clients). This gives every peer
access to the DHT without requiring a relay. From the DHT, the peer
discovers relay peers. From the relay, the peer reaches the mesh.
This is a 10-line configuration change, not a design change. But no
expert mentioned it.

### The Savant missed: the priority queue thundering herd in small meshes

The Savant's replication work loop uses most_under_replicated() to
select matter for replication. In a mesh with 2-3 clusters, all
gateways have similar filter views (filters converge within a few
heartbeat intervals on a small mesh). All gateways compute the same
most_under_replicated() result. All gateways select the same target.
All gateways initiate the same transfer.

For a 2-cluster mesh with 1000 under-replicated blobs, both gateways
evaluate most_under_replicated() and select the same blob (the one
with the lowest replica count). Both initiate a transfer of the same
blob to the same target. 100% redundancy.

Expert A's probabilistic approach avoids this because the BLAKE3
roll is peer-specific (includes my_dk in the hash input). With 2
peers, each has a ~50% probability of initiating each transfer.
Expected redundancy: 50%. Better than the Savant's 100%.

The Savant should add a peer-specific tiebreaker to
most_under_replicated(). For example: sort by (replica_count, BLAKE3(
blob_id || my_dk) % 65536). This ensures different peers prioritize
different blobs when replica counts are equal. This is a 3-line
change to the sort comparator but eliminates the thundering herd in
small meshes.

## Section 5: The Hidden Interaction -- WiFi Power Management and AEAD Nonce Safety

No expert addresses the interaction between WiFi power management and
the AEAD nonce safety mechanism (NonceSafety).

The NonceSafety specification says: "Before using a nonce, the next
batch of nonce values must be persisted to flash. If the persist
fails, the session is halted." The persist frequency is determined by
the nonce batch size. The design says batch size 256 (persist every
256 frames).

On WiFi with power management, frames arrive in bursts (500 frames
after a 500ms power-save interval). The event loop processes 32
frames per arena batch, draining 500 frames in 16 iterations. Each
iteration may generate response frames that consume nonces. If 500
incoming frames trigger 500 response frames (heartbeat responses,
SACKs, application responses), the outgoing nonce advances by 500 in
~800us.

The nonce batch persist (every 256 nonces) must complete before the
nonce advances past the persisted range. If the event loop consumes
500 nonces in 800us, it must persist twice (at nonce 256 and nonce
512). Each persist is a flash write. On SD card flash: ~2-10ms per
write (depending on SD card wear level and queue depth). Two persists:
4-20ms.

The event loop cannot proceed until the persist completes (the nonce
is not safe to use until persisted). This creates a 4-20ms stall in
the event loop during every WiFi power-save burst. On a Pi with an
SD card: 20ms stall. On a Pi with eMMC: 4ms stall.

This stall is additive with the WiFi burst processing time (800us).
Total event loop stall during WiFi burst: 800us + 20ms = ~21ms.
During this 21ms, no heartbeats are processed, no SACKs are sent,
and no timer deadlines are checked. At 1-second heartbeat intervals,
a 21ms stall is negligible. But the stall repeats every 500ms
(every WiFi power-save cycle). The event loop spends 21/500 = 4.2%
of its time stalled on nonce persists triggered by WiFi bursts. On
a Pi Zero with limited CPU, this 4.2% may push the system from Idle
to Active metabolic state, changing heartbeat intervals and LEDBAT
parameters.

The solution is to decouple nonce persistence from the event loop.
The nonce batch can be persisted asynchronously (fire-and-forget
write), with the event loop using an in-memory monotonic counter that
is always ahead of the persisted value. If the system crashes before
the persist completes, the nonce gap (up to 256 values) is lost, but
the nonce monotonicity invariant is preserved because the next
startup reads the persisted value and starts from there.

Expert B's sans-IO model should include a NoncePersistRequest output
event and a NoncePersistComplete input event, analogous to the
CryptoRequest/CryptoResult pattern. This decouples the nonce persist
from the event loop, eliminating the 4-20ms stall.

## Section 7: The Uncomfortable Conclusion

The design is built for a LAN that behaves like Ethernet. The
deployment target is WiFi. These are not the same thing.

Every mechanism that uses fixed timeouts (heartbeat thresholds, scar
thresholds, amplification prevention windows, LEDBAT measurements)
needs a WiFi-aware variant. The Conductor's metabolic state should
include a "WiFi quality" dimension that adjusts timeouts based on
observed packet loss and jitter. This is not a separate mechanism; it
is a new resource dimension in the existing GovernedResource framework.

The minimum change is a WiFi quality estimator that feeds into the
Conductor:

```
wifi_quality = observed_delivery_ratio over last 30 seconds
// 1.0 = no loss (Ethernet-like)
// 0.85 = 15% loss (typical household WiFi)
// 0.60 = 40% loss (interference burst)

Adjustments when wifi_quality < 0.90:
- Intra-cluster scar threshold: 30 * (1 / wifi_quality) = ~35 at 85%
- Heartbeat interval: base_interval * (1 / wifi_quality^2) = ~1.38s at 85%
- LEDBAT OWD correction: measured_owd - wifi_buffer_estimate
- Amplification window: base_window * 2
- Gateway election dampening: 10 * (1 / wifi_quality) = ~12 seconds
```

This adds one PhysicalBoundary gate (wifi_quality measurement from
OS), one GovernedResource dimension, and ~20 lines of adjustment
logic in the Conductor. The adjustment formulas are fixed-point
(Q16.16 division). The total specification impact is ~50 lines of
body text.

Without this change, the design will work well on Ethernet test
benches and fail intermittently on the household WiFi networks where
it will actually be deployed. The failure mode (scar accumulation,
gateway thrashing, one-directional relay flow) is subtle and
cumulative, making it hard to diagnose and easy to blame on "WiFi
being WiFi." The correct response is to design for WiFi, not to
blame WiFi.
