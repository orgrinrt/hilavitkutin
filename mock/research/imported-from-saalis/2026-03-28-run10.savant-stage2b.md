## 2. What They All Missed

### 2.1 The Convergence-Health Feedback Loop Is Unstable

Every reviewer touched HealthPipeline. Every reviewer touched Convergence. Nobody connected them into a feedback loop and asked whether the loop is stable.

**Crystallographic:** The HealthPipeline is a bond that feeds into Convergence gating (mesh uses health to decide when convergence can proceed). Convergence changes network topology (re-encryption, key rotation, gateway election). Topology changes alter heartbeat delivery ratios. Delivery ratios feed back into HealthPipeline. This is a closed loop: HealthPipeline -> Convergence -> Topology Change -> Heartbeat Disruption -> HealthPipeline. The loop has no damping specification.

**Concrete mechanism:** During convergence Front 2, the gateway may change (dominance resolution after fencing). Gateway change disrupts active connections (old gateway's sessions must migrate to new gateway). Connection migration causes transient packet loss. The HealthPipeline's fast EMA (alpha=0.1, ~10 heartbeats) reacts to this transient loss within 50 seconds (10 heartbeats at 5s intervals in Active mode). If the fast EMA drops below 0.50 (Bad), source selection excludes the peer. If enough peers are excluded from source selection, matter transfers stall. Stalled matter transfers during convergence Front 3 (re-encryption) delay re-encryption completion. Delayed re-encryption keeps the convergence gate open. Open convergence gate maintains the metabolic override (Active or Stress). The override prevents the system from returning to Idle, sustaining higher heartbeat frequency, which sustains higher observation volume, which sustains the health perturbation.

**Emergent behavior:** The loop can oscillate. Convergence disrupts health. Disrupted health delays convergence. The delay extends the period of disruption. The system reaches a steady state only when (a) the health perturbation from convergence is smaller than the EMA's noise floor, or (b) the convergence gate has a timeout that closes regardless of health state. The current spec has wavefront deadlines (timeouts), which serve as a damping mechanism. But the interaction between wavefront deadline expiry and health score recovery is unspecified. If the wavefront deadline fires while health scores are still depressed, what happens? The spec says "continue with whatever state is available." But "whatever state is available" with depressed health scores means source selection is constrained, re-encryption targets may be unhealthy, and the gateway may be suboptimal. The system recovers, but slowly and nondeterministically.

Nobody flagged this because nobody traced a full closed loop through the design. The SpecAuditor analyzed mechanisms in isolation. The DepAnalyst traced dependencies but not feedback paths. The GapHunter walked through implementations linearly. The Devil's Advocate questioned assumptions but not dynamics. Feedback loops are invisible to all four methodologies.

### 2.2 The Scar System Is a Slow-Acting Poison During Sustained Partitions

**Crystallographic:** Scars are accumulated observations of peer misbehavior, decaying linearly over 180 days. The dominance formula uses `weighted_scar_count` to demote scarred peers. During a sustained partition (say, 30 days), peers in the smaller partition accumulate PartitionAbsence scars from the larger partition's perspective. PartitionAbsence is excluded from dominance scoring. But other scar types are not excluded.

**Concrete mechanism:** Consider: during the partition, Peer A in the smaller partition has a local flash storage issue. It accumulates IntegrityFailure scars (detected by local verification, not by remote observation). These scars are legitimate. When the partition heals and convergence begins, the larger partition's peers receive the scar GSet merge. Peer A now has IntegrityFailure scars visible to the whole mesh. These scars affect dominance scoring. But the flash issue was repaired on day 5 of the 30-day partition. The scars from days 1-5 persist for 180 days, affecting Peer A's dominance score long after the hardware issue was resolved.

**Emergent behavior:** In a household mesh, hardware issues are common (cheap eMMC, thermal throttling, WiFi interference) and temporary (user replaces cable, moves device, reboots). The 180-day scar decay means temporary issues have long tails. A peer that had a bad week six months ago is still paying for it in dominance scoring. This interacts with the "weighted_scar_count" exponent in the dominance formula: `2^max(0, weighted_scar_count - 10)` applies exponential demotion once scars exceed 10. A burst of 15 IntegrityFailure scars from a week-long flash issue creates a 2^5 = 32x dominance penalty that persists for months.

The SpecAuditor flagged the 180-day decay as UNJUSTIFIED (M12) but did not trace the interaction with dominance scoring's exponential penalty. The combination of linear decay and exponential penalty creates a cliff effect: below 10 scars, the penalty is manageable. Above 10, it is catastrophic. A peer that crosses the threshold during a temporary issue may never recover admin status, even after the hardware is repaired and no new scars accumulate, because the exponential penalty delays recovery far beyond the linear decay timeline.

### 2.3 The Missing Seat-During-Convergence Contract Cascades to User Experience

I flagged this in Stage 1 (V1: "What happens to seat sessions during convergence?"). Every other reviewer flagged seat emptiness as a specification gap. Nobody connected the two into a user-visible failure.

**Crystallographic:** The seat cell is isolated from the mesh cell. The HostPeer is the only bond. During convergence, the HostPeer is consumed by mesh operations (control log merge, dominance resolution, re-encryption coordination). If the HostPeer is also the gateway, it is doing gateway work. The seat's requests queue behind mesh work.

**Concrete mechanism:** A user opens the web UI (seat) to browse their game library during a partition heal (convergence). The HostPeer is in Active metabolic state (convergence override). It is processing Front 1 (control log merge). The user's QueryEntities request arrives. The HostPeer's request handler is synchronous (saalis architecture: sync core, async shell). The control log merge is holding the state lock. The QueryEntities request blocks until the merge releases the lock. The merge takes 30 seconds (large GSet with many entries). The user sees a 30-second hang.

**Emergent behavior:** The user thinks the application is broken. They force-quit the seat. The seat reconnects. The reconnection triggers a SchemaSync. The SchemaSync requires the HostPeer to serialize its protobuf schema. The HostPeer is still in convergence. Another delay. The user gives up. This is the worst kind of failure for a consumer appliance: the system is technically functioning but the user experience is degraded to the point of unusability.

The fix is not just "define seat behavior during convergence." The fix is to ensure that the HostPeer can serve seat requests concurrently with convergence operations. This requires specifying which state the seat can access during convergence (read-only snapshot? stale data? or blocked?), which is a design decision, not a specification gap. Nobody framed it as a design decision because everybody treated seat emptiness as a documentation problem.

### 2.4 The Wire Contract Gap Is Not Just About Interop

The Devil's Advocate correctly identified that wire contracts are missing and that this will cause interop failure. But the Devil's Advocate framed it as a two-team problem. There is a deeper structural issue: the absence of wire contracts means the lattice has no rigidity.

**Crystallographic:** A crystal's rigidity comes from the precise positions of atoms in the unit cell. If the atomic positions are specified only as "somewhere in this region," the crystal is amorphous, not crystalline. The defined concepts are the regions. The wire contracts are the atomic positions. Without wire contracts, the lattice I analyzed in Stage 1 is a glass, not a crystal. My bond strength analysis assumed precise bonds. If the bonds are imprecise (prose invariants without byte layouts), every bond strength assessment is overstated. The "strong bonds" in my Stage 1 table are strong only if the defined concepts resolve to unique byte sequences. Currently, they do not.

**Concrete mechanism:** FrameKey's invariant says "Per-frame key derived from epoch key via HKDF-Expand." This is a strong bond if HKDF-Expand means one specific construction. But as the Devil's Advocate showed, HKDF-Expand vs HKDF (Extract+Expand) is a real ambiguity. My bond strength analysis did not detect this because I was analyzing invariant prose, not byte-level compatibility.

**Emergent behavior:** Every "Strong" bond in my Stage 1 table must be downgraded to "Medium" until wire contracts exist. This is not a findings update; it is a methodological correction. My crystallographic analysis was operating on an amorphous material and treating it as crystalline. The lattice is less sound than I reported.

## 3. Novel Mechanisms

### 3.1 Convergence Damping Coefficient

**Crystallographic:** Insert a damping element at the HealthPipeline-Convergence feedback junction. The damping coefficient suppresses health score perturbations that originate from convergence-induced topology changes, preventing the feedback loop from oscillating.

**Concrete mechanism:** When convergence is active (any gate open), the HealthPipeline applies a convergence damping mask: health observations during convergence are weighted at 0.3x their normal weight for fast EMA computation and 0.1x for slow EMA computation. The damping mask lifts when all convergence gates close. This means that health perturbations caused by gateway migration, key rotation, and re-encryption are registered (they are real) but attenuated (they are expected). The specific coefficients (0.3x fast, 0.1x slow) ensure that genuine failures during convergence (a peer actually going offline, not just experiencing transient disruption) still trigger health state transitions, but slower.

**Emergent behavior:** The damping coefficient converts the convergence-health feedback loop from a potentially oscillating system into a critically damped one. After convergence completes and the mask lifts, the fast EMA recovers to true health within ~10 heartbeats. The slow EMA recovers within ~100 heartbeats. During recovery, source selection uses the slow EMA (which was less perturbed due to lower weight), providing stable selection decisions. The system exhibits overshoot suppression: instead of "convergence disrupts health disrupts convergence," the pattern becomes "convergence disrupts health (attenuated) and convergence proceeds to completion."

### 3.2 Scar Severity Tiering

**Crystallographic:** The current scar system treats all scars as equal weight inputs to dominance scoring. The exponential penalty at `weighted_scar_count > 10` creates a cliff. Replace the uniform weight with a severity tier that distinguishes transient hardware issues from persistent misbehavior.

**Concrete mechanism:** Three scar tiers:
```
Tier 1 (transient): IntegrityFailure, ConnectivityFlap
  - Decay: 90 days (not 180)
  - Weight in dominance: 0.5x

Tier 2 (concerning): DeliveryFailure, ResourceExhaustion
  - Decay: 180 days
  - Weight in dominance: 1.0x

Tier 3 (severe): RevocationNonCompliance, CryptoViolation
  - Decay: 365 days
  - Weight in dominance: 2.0x
```
The `weighted_scar_count` formula becomes `sum(scar.weight * scar.decay_factor)` instead of `count(scars) * decay_factor`. The exponential threshold changes from 10 scars to 10.0 weighted scar units.

**Emergent behavior:** A peer with 15 Tier-1 scars from a week-long flash issue accumulates 15 * 0.5 = 7.5 weighted units. This is below the exponential threshold (10.0), so no cliff penalty. The same peer would need 20 Tier-1 scars to cross the threshold. Meanwhile, 6 Tier-3 scars (actual crypto violations) accumulate 6 * 2.0 = 12.0 weighted units, immediately triggering exponential demotion. The system differentiates between "cheap hardware being cheap" (household reality) and "peer is actually untrustworthy" (security concern).

### 3.3 The Lattice Rigidity Layer (Wire Contracts as Crystal Bonds)

**Crystallographic:** The Devil's Advocate's Wire Contract proposal adds the missing atomic positions to the crystal. But the proposal is flat: add wire contracts to all 40 defined concepts. The lattice structure tells us where rigidity matters most and where it can be deferred. Wire contracts at grain boundaries are load-bearing. Wire contracts at internal-only concepts are nice-to-have.

**Concrete mechanism:** Prioritize wire contracts by bond type:
```
Priority 1 (grain boundary bonds -- 2 documents share ownership):
  HeartbeatExtension, ConvergenceGate, SourceSelectionPredicate,
  DomainSeparatorRegistry entries
  ~8 concepts, ~200 lines of wire spec

Priority 2 (cross-document strong bonds -- consumer uses invariants):
  FrameKey, TransportAEAD, EpochRatchet, BroadcastEncryption,
  StorageAEAD, Frame, Connection, Session
  ~12 concepts, ~400 lines of wire spec

Priority 3 (document-internal concepts):
  ControlLog, GovernedResource, Stash, SeatSession, etc.
  ~20 concepts, ~400 lines of wire spec
```
The total is the same ~1,000 lines the Devil's Advocate estimated, but the ordering ensures that the most structurally critical wire contracts are specified first. If implementation pressure forces a partial specification, the grain boundary bonds are rigid and the internal concepts can be deferred.

**Emergent behavior:** By specifying grain boundary wire contracts first, the two-team interop failure scenario is mitigated at the most vulnerable interfaces. Two teams can diverge on internal implementations (how they store scars, how they structure their PI controller) while remaining compatible at the grain boundaries (heartbeat extension format, convergence gate protocol, frame format). This converts the Devil's Advocate's all-or-nothing proposal into a progressive rigidification of the lattice, starting from the grain boundaries inward.

### 3.4 Seat Isolation Valve

**Crystallographic:** The seat cell is isolated from the mesh cell by design (DepAnalyst confirms: "Clean isolation. The host peer serves as the boundary. This is well-designed."). But isolation without a pressure valve means that mesh pressure (convergence) backs up into the seat's response latency. The valve allows the HostPeer to serve seat requests from a snapshot while mesh operations proceed on the live state.

**Concrete mechanism:** The HostPeer maintains two state views:
```
live_state:     mutable, used by mesh operations (convergence, gossip, health)
snapshot_state: read-only copy, used by seat requests during convergence
```
When convergence begins, the HostPeer takes a snapshot of the current entity metadata and stash status. Seat queries (QueryEntities, GetEntityDetail, GetStashStatus) are served from `snapshot_state`. Seat mutations (StartDownload, ExecuteInstruction) are queued and replayed after convergence completes. The snapshot is invalidated when convergence ends.

Invariant: seat reads are never blocked by convergence. Seat writes are deferred, not rejected. The user sees stale-but-responsive data instead of correct-but-frozen data.

**Emergent behavior:** The user's experience during convergence degrades gracefully: they can browse their library (snapshot), but new downloads do not start until convergence completes. If convergence takes 30 seconds, the user sees "download queued" rather than a 30-second hang. If convergence takes 5 minutes (large partition heal), the user sees stale data that progressively updates when convergence completes. The seat isolation valve converts a user-hostile failure (hang) into a user-tolerable degradation (staleness).

Grain boundaries that become internal (e.g., if mesh absorbs relay) lose their brittleness. Grain boundaries that remain external (e.g., crypto-protocol, which the Devil's Advocate also predicts will merge) gain the wire contract rigidity. The merging of crates is a simplification of the lattice, not a destruction. Fewer grain boundaries means fewer cleavage planes. If the lattice consolidates to three crates, my cleavage plane analysis becomes simpler, not invalid.

However, the Devil's Advocate is right that I assumed a seven-cell lattice. If the lattice is actually three cells, my vacancy analysis changes. V1 (seat has no bond to mesh) may become an internal gap within the merged `verkko-matter-seat` cell, which is easier to fix. V3 (matter has no bond to protocol) may dissolve if matter merges into the mesh-relay cell that already depends on protocol.

I am not conceding that the seven-crate decomposition will fail. I am conceding that my analysis should have been robust to lattice consolidation. It was not. I analyzed the structure as given without asking whether the structure was stable.

### 4.2 Assumption 2: "Defined Concepts Are Sufficient"

The Devil's Advocate is right and I was wrong. My bond strength analysis treated defined concept invariants as precise bonds. They are not. They are prose descriptions that constrain the semantic space without constraining the byte space. The two-team interop scenario demonstrates that my "Strong" bond ratings are conditional on wire contract existence. Without wire contracts, every bond is Medium at best.

The fix is section 3.3 above (Lattice Rigidity Layer). The wire contracts must exist for my crystallographic analysis to be meaningful. Without them, I was analyzing a glass and calling it a crystal.

### 4.3 Assumption 3: "Health Scalar Is Sufficient"

The Devil's Advocate's example (high uptime, high packet loss peer elected as gateway during convergence) is a real failure scenario. But the proposed fix (add health dimensions) is too aggressive. Adding dimensions (throughput, latency, uptime as separate signals) to the HealthPipeline's output turns every consumer into a multi-dimensional optimizer. Gateway election must optimize for a different combination of dimensions than source selection, which must optimize for a different combination than metabolic state transition.

The crystallographic fix is not more dimensions. It is purpose-specific projection functions. The HealthPipeline produces a multi-dimensional health vector internally (it already computes fast EMA, slow EMA, scar count, and observation mix). The fix is to expose purpose-specific projections:

```
health.for_source_selection()   -> weighted toward throughput, packet loss
health.for_gateway_election()   -> weighted toward uptime, connectivity
health.for_metabolic_state()    -> weighted toward resource pressure (CPU, flash, bandwidth)
health.for_convergence_gate()   -> weighted toward reachability, uptime
```

Each projection is a defined concept with its own invariants. The internal representation remains a single pipeline. The consumers see purpose-fitted scalars. This preserves the pipeline's simplicity while giving each consumer a meaningful signal.

## 6. The Design Synthesis Through the Crystallographic Lens

The lattice has seven unit cells. The core three (crypto, protocol, mesh) form a dense, well-bonded region with high internal symmetry. The relay cell bonds adequately to the core but through informal references that become grain boundaries under evolution stress. The seat and matter cells are partially hollow, creating structural weakness at the lattice periphery.

After reading all eight documents from the other reviewers, the crystal's true state is:

**The lattice is amorphous, not crystalline.** My Stage 1 analysis treated it as crystalline. The Devil's Advocate proved it is not. Without wire contracts, the atomic positions within each unit cell are imprecise. The bonds between cells are semantic agreements, not physical constraints. The crystal will solidify only when wire contracts pin the atomic positions. Until then, my analysis describes the intended crystal structure, not the actual material.

**Three feedback loops exist. One is unspecified.** The convergence-health loop (section 2.1) has no damping specification. The scar-dominance loop (section 2.2) has a cliff that punishes transient failures disproportionately. The gossip-convergence loop (gossip mode transitions during convergence) is well-specified with explicit gates and ACK mechanisms. Two of three loops are potential oscillation sources.

**The cleavage plane hierarchy must be revised.** My Stage 1 ranking was:
1. C3: Unregistered domain separator (catastrophic, silent)
2. C1: Heartbeat format change (high blast radius)
3. C2: New convergence front (medium-high)
4. C4: MetabolicState expansion (medium)

Revised ranking after cross-review:
1. C1: Heartbeat format change (high blast radius, 5 documents, grain boundary without defined concept)
2. C2: New convergence front (medium-high, but named fronts fix this)
3. C4: MetabolicState expansion (medium, but abstract governor interface fixes this)
4. C3: Unregistered domain separator (medium, process guard not design defect)

The ordering change reflects the concession in section 5.4. The heartbeat interface is the most load-bearing bond in the lattice and it lacks a defined concept at its grain boundary. This is the #1 structural priority.

**The revised priority repair list:**
1. S1 (HeartbeatExtension defined concept) -- the highest-traffic grain boundary needs formalization
2. S6 (ConvergenceGate defined concept) -- the most coupled grain boundary needs a single point of truth
3. S3 (Named convergence fronts) -- prevents cascade from insertion

5. Wire contracts at grain boundaries (section 3.3, Priority 1) -- lattice rigidity
6. S5 (Seat-mesh mediation contract with isolation valve) -- fills the largest vacancy with a user-experience-aware design
7. S4 (Source selection health threshold) -- fills a functional gap

9. Scar severity tiering (section 3.2) -- prevents cliff effects from transient hardware issues

## 7. Anti-Agreement: The Devil's Advocate Is Wrong About Seven Crates

The Devil's Advocate claims "the probability of the seven-crate decomposition surviving implementation intact is low" and predicts consolidation to three crates based on coupling analysis. The evidence cited: "4 of 6 pairwise document boundaries have implicit coupling edges."

This analysis conflates coupling with merger necessity. Coupling edges exist in every modular system. The existence of implicit edges between protocol and mesh does not mean they should be one crate. The protocol layer is sans-IO; the mesh layer is stateful and topology-aware. These are fundamentally different concerns that happen to interact at the heartbeat and metabolic state boundaries. Merging them would create a single crate that is simultaneously responsible for frame parsing, state machine management, gossip protocol, convergence sequencing, health scoring, and resource governance. That is not simplification; it is a monolith.

The Devil's Advocate's merger prediction (crypto+protocol, mesh+relay, matter+seat) is based on coupling density, not on separation of concerns. From a crystallographic perspective, coupling density means the grain boundaries are load-bearing, not that the cells should merge. Two cells with many bonds between them are strongly coupled. Strongly coupled is good. It means the lattice has high fracture toughness at that boundary. Merging the cells destroys the boundary, which means the internal structure loses the grain boundary's documentation and API discipline.

The correct response to coupling is not merger; it is formalization. HeartbeatExtension formalizes the protocol-mesh boundary. ConvergenceGate formalizes the mesh-relay boundary. Named fronts stabilize the cross-references. After formalization, the seven cells are seven crates with well-defined API surfaces. The Devil's Advocate's prediction holds only if formalization fails.

## 8. Savant Re-Sharpening: Naming Domestications

The experts formalized several of my proposals. In the process, they filed down edges. Here are the domestications I am naming and the sharp edges I am restoring.

### 8.1 HeartbeatExtension Was Domesticated to "a defined concept"

My Stage 1 proposal S1 described HeartbeatExtension as owning the extension region layout with an explicit size bound, a field allocation table, and a reserved space count. The SpecAuditor and DepAnalyst both "accept" S1 as a defined concept. But their acceptance strips the emergent behavior component: the HeartbeatExtension defined concept is not just a type. It is the single synchronization point for six different consumers (failure detection, health scoring, epoch sync, filter divergence detection, gossip mode switching, metabolic state observation). The defined concept must carry an invariant that states this: "HeartbeatExtension is the sole carrier of mesh-layer state in the transport layer. Any mesh-layer state that must be observed by remote peers must be carried in the HeartbeatExtension. There is no secondary observation channel."

Without this invariant, a future designer might add a separate mesh state channel (a control frame that carries filter status, for example). This would create a second observation path, violating the current design's guarantee that heartbeat delivery ratio is the complete signal for health measurement. The experts accepted the structural fix (defined concept with size bounds) but dropped the design constraint (sole observation channel). I restore the sharp edge.

### 8.2 ConvergenceGate Was Domesticated to "gate conditions"

My Stage 1 proposal S6 described ConvergenceGate with four boolean predicates. The DepAnalyst accepted it as "formalizing the mesh-relay convergence interface." But the emergent behavior I described was the key point: relay no longer reaches across to protocol (VitalQueue depth) directly; it consumes the gate condition from mesh. This is a decoupling mechanism, not just a documentation convenience. The gate acts as a firewall: relay cannot observe protocol's internal state. It can only observe mesh's summary gate. If the VitalQueue threshold changes from 25% to 30%, only mesh's gate logic updates. Relay is insulated.

The experts treated ConvergenceGate as an organizational improvement. It is a coupling barrier. Without the barrier property being explicit, a future designer might "optimize" by having relay check VitalQueue directly (bypassing the gate). The invariant must state: "Consumers of ConvergenceGate must not inspect the inputs to the gate predicates. The gate is the interface. The inputs are implementation details of the gate owner."

### 8.3 The Domain Separator Lint Was Domesticated to "ops build plan"

Both DepAnalyst and SpecAuditor moved S2 from a design-level fix to an implementation-level fix (ops build plan). They are correct about timing (v1 is complete, the lint is for v2). But they domesticated the failure mode analysis. My Stage 1 called it "catastrophic, silent." The DepAnalyst called it "a developer compliance issue." The SpecAuditor called it "process safeguard."

The sharp edge: silent mesh partition is not a developer compliance issue. It is a protocol design failure. The protocol produces a catastrophic outcome from a small input error (one unregistered BLAKE3 call). Protocols that produce catastrophic outcomes from small errors are badly designed, regardless of whether the error is "a process failure." TLS 1.0's padding oracle was "a developer error." It was also a protocol design failure because the protocol amplified a small implementation error into a complete security bypass. The DomainSeparatorRegistry has the same amplification property: one missed registration produces silent mesh partition.

I conceded the priority ranking (moved from #1 to #4). I do not concede the classification. This is a design defect, not merely a process concern. The fix belongs in the design specification (crypto defines a registration mechanism, not just a registration rule) even if the enforcement is implemented in ops. The distinction matters: a rule that says "you must register" can be ignored. A mechanism that makes unregistered usage impossible cannot.

## Summary

The crystal has seven cells. The core is dense and well-bonded. The periphery is hollow. The lattice is amorphous (lacking wire contracts) but has a clear crystalline target structure. Three feedback loops exist; one is undamped. The scar system's exponential penalty creates a cliff that punishes transient hardware failures disproportionately. The seat's isolation from mesh creates a user experience hazard during convergence.

The priority sequence is: formalize grain boundaries (HeartbeatExtension, ConvergenceGate, named fronts), add wire contracts at grain boundaries first, fill the seat and matter vacancies with user-experience-aware designs, stabilize the convergence-health feedback loop, and tier the scar severity system.

The lattice will crystallize when wire contracts pin the atomic positions. Until then, it is a glass with good intentions.
