# Run 10: Tension Map (Stage 1.5)

**Date:** 2026-03-28
**Input:** specauditor, depanalyst, gaphunter, savant Stage 1 reviews

---

## Tension 1: Document coupling cycles vs. deliberate interface split

**The conflict.** DepAnalyst flags two implicit back-edges as structural problems requiring resolution: `verkko-crypto -> verkko-mesh` (crypto body defers enrollment and PCS distribution to mesh) and `verkko-protocol -> verkko-mesh` (MetabolicState defined concept defers its driver to Conductor in mesh). DepAnalyst calls the protocol/mesh case a "partial definitional cycle" and a "tight coupling point." Savant sees the same topology and calls it a strength: "crypto -> protocol. Clean dependency. Protocol uses only crypto's invariants. No leakage in either direction. This is the lattice's backbone." On the heartbeat extension split specifically, DepAnalyst rates it as clean interface segregation ("mesh fills in the semantics, this is clean"), while Savant rates the same interface as its most load-bearing grain boundary (G1) requiring its own defined concept to harden.

**Question for cross-review.** Are the back-edges in crypto and protocol genuine coupling defects that must be resolved by moving or merging concepts, or are they acceptable forward-references that document consumers should tolerate? And is the heartbeat extension split a design strength or a grain boundary that needs a HeartbeatExtension defined concept to stabilize it?

## Tension 2: Severity triage — missing wire formats vs. missing enforcement mechanisms

**The conflict.** GapHunter and SpecAuditor both cover the same ground but reach different severity rankings. GapHunter rates the absence of stream multiplexing wire format (no stream_id field) as BLOCKING and treats it as an implementation stopper. SpecAuditor rates the same gap (P3) as HIGH but frames it as a specification completeness issue that needs a minimum body, not necessarily an immediate blocker. GapHunter is similarly more alarmed about seat gaps (S-1 through S-8, all rated BLOCKING) than Savant, who calls seat "~15% filled" but treats it as a structural placeholder rather than an emergency. Conversely, Savant rates the DomainSeparatorRegistry enforcement gap (G4/C3) as the single most dangerous defect in the entire design — "catastrophic, silent" — while SpecAuditor covers the same gap as finding C15 at LOW severity.

**Question for cross-review.** Which severity scale is correct for the cross-review to use: GapHunter's implementer-halts framing (blocking = cannot write a line of code), SpecAuditor's interoperability framing (critical = two implementations diverge), or Savant's emergent-risk framing (catastrophic = silent failure under stress)? The three scales produce different priority orderings for the same findings.

## Tension 3: MetabolicState ownership — protocol defines, mesh drives, no one owns the predicates

**The conflict.** DepAnalyst identifies that MetabolicState transition predicates live in neither document: "The design splits the mechanism from the driver across two documents that already depend on each other in the other direction." SpecAuditor finding P13 agrees the predicates are missing but offers two acceptable fixes: add them to protocol, or explicitly delegate them to mesh's Conductor with a cross-reference. GapHunter (P-6, rated BLOCKING elsewhere) does not specifically flag MetabolicState transitions as a blocking gap. Savant calls the mesh/protocol MetabolicState coupling a "medium bond" and does not list it as a priority repair.

**Question for cross-review.** Should MetabolicState transition predicates be owned by verkko-protocol (making the state machine self-contained) or by verkko-mesh: Conductor (accepting that the driver defines the triggers)? The answer determines whether the protocol/mesh implicit cycle is a true coupling defect or an intentional layering choice that needs explicit documentation.

## Tension 4: verkko-matter's undeclared dependency on verkko-protocol

**The conflict.** DepAnalyst flags a hidden edge H7 (matter -> protocol via LEDBAT) as a medium-severity undeclared dependency requiring a fix to the Dependencies section. Savant flags the same relationship as vacancy V3 ("Matter has no direct bond to Protocol") and calls it a structural weakness: matter transfer priority tier, bulk stream assignment, and LEDBAT pacing are all unspecified bonds. SpecAuditor does not raise the missing LEDBAT declaration as a finding at all. GapHunter does not directly raise it either, but flags the BandwidthPolicy body as incomplete from an implementation standpoint (P-7, LEDBAT variant unspecified).

**Question for cross-review.** Is the matter-protocol relationship a declaration paperwork fix (add verkko-protocol to matter's Dependencies), a full vacancy that requires specifying how MatterTransfer maps to protocol's Stream and VitalQueue, or both? And does resolving this require adding matter to the topological sort at level 5 (after relay and protocol), which currently places it at level 4?

## Tension 5: Empty cells — design intent vs. specification debt

**The conflict.** Savant characterizes verkko-seat and verkko-matter as structurally hollow ("15% filled" and "40% filled") and treats this as a known structural state that needs minimum lattice content to be sound. Savant's tone is diagnostic: "The cell exists as a structural placeholder." GapHunter treats the same emptiness as an immediate implementation crisis: seat alone has eight BLOCKING gaps, every one of which halts coding. SpecAuditor finds 6 seat-related findings and 7+ matter-related findings, all HIGH or MEDIUM. DepAnalyst rates seat's interface sufficiency as SUFFICIENT for its declared dependencies (crypto, protocol) — seat's problem is not incorrect dependencies but absent body content.

The underlying disagreement is whether a partially specified document is a known design debt to be addressed in later rounds, or whether it is already blocking the cross-review from making meaningful progress on the documents that depend on seat and matter behavior.

**Question for cross-review.** Should the cross-review treat seat and matter as out-of-scope pending a separate specification pass, or should Stage 2 reviewers attempt to resolve the seat and matter gaps using inferences from the existing documents? And is DepAnalyst's "interface sufficiency: SUFFICIENT" rating for seat a reassurance or a warning sign that the gaps are invisible to dependency analysis?
